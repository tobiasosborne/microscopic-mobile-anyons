# A mathematical theory of gapless edges of 2d topological orders. Part I

Liang Kong*<sup>a</sup>* , Hao Zheng*<sup>a</sup>*,*<sup>b</sup>* [1](#page-0-0)

*<sup>a</sup>* Shenzhen Institute for Quantum Science and Engineering, and Department of Physics, Southern University of Science and Technology, Shenzhen 518055, China

> *<sup>b</sup>* Department of Mathematics, Peking University Beijing 100871, China

#### **Abstract**

This is the first part of a two-part work on a unified mathematical theory of gapped and gapless edges of 2d topological orders. We analyze all the possible observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge of a 2d topological order, and show that these observables form an enriched unitary fusion category, the Drinfeld center of which is precisely the unitary modular tensor category associated to the bulk. This mathematical description of a chiral gapless edge automatically includes that of a gapped edge (i.e. a unitary fusion category) as a special case. Therefore, we obtain a unified mathematical description and a classification of both gapped and chiral gapless edges of a given 2d topological order. In the process of our analysis, we encounter an interesting and reoccurring phenomenon: spatial fusion anomaly, which leads us to propose the Principle of Universality at RG fixed points for all quantum field theories. Our theory also implies that all chiral gapless edges can be obtained from a so-called topological Wick rotations. This fact leads us to propose, at the end of this work, a surprising correspondence between gapped and gapless phases in all dimensions.

## <span id="page-1-0"></span>**1 Introduction**

Topological phases of matter have attracted a lot of attentions in recent years among physicists because they go beyond Landaus paradigm of phases and phase transitions (see a recent review [\[W5\]](#page-51-0) and references therein). In this work and part II [\[KZ4\]](#page-50-0), we develop a unified mathematical theory of the gapped and gapless edges of 2d topological orders. Some results of this two-part work were announced in [\[KZ3\]](#page-50-1) without providing the details. In Part I, we focus on chiral gapless edges.

Throughout this work, we use "*n*d" to mean the spatial dimension and "*n*+1D" to mean the spacetime dimension, and by a "2d topological order", we mean an anomaly-free 2d topological order without symmetry. We use "Theorem" to represent a mathematical (rigorous) result and "Theoremph" to summarize important physical (unrigorous) results.

Topological orders are the universal classes of gapped local Hamiltonian lattice models at zero temperature. Since the system is gapped, correlation functions decay exponentially. In the long wave length limit, the only local observables are topological excitations. It was known that a 2d topological order is determined by its (particle-like) topological excitations uniquely up to *E*<sup>8</sup> states. Mathematically, the fusion-braiding properties of the topological excitations in a 2d topological order can be described by a unitary modular tensor category (UMTC) (see for example [\[FrRS,](#page-47-0) [FG\]](#page-47-1) and a review [\[Ki1,](#page-49-0) Appendix E]). Therefore, a 2d topological order is described mathematically by a pair (C, *c*), where C is the UMTC of topological excitations and *c* is the chiral central charge. If a topological order (C, 0) admits a gapped edge, it is called a nonchiral topological order. The mathematical theory of gapped edges is known. More precisely, a gapped edge can be mathematically described by a unitary fusion category (UFC) M such that its Drinfeld center Z(M) coincides with C [\[KK,](#page-49-1) [FSV,](#page-48-0) [Ko6\]](#page-49-2). The fact that the bulk phase is determined by an edge as its Drinfeld center is called the *boundary-bulk relation*.

When a 2d topological order is chiral, it has topologically protected gapless edges [\[Ha,](#page-48-1) [W1,](#page-51-1) [MR,](#page-50-2) [W2\]](#page-51-2) (see reviews [\[W2,](#page-51-2) [W3,](#page-51-3) [NSSFS\]](#page-50-3) and references therein). Observables on a gapless edge is significantly richer than those on a gapped edge because gapless edge modes are described by a 1+1D conformal field theory (CFT) [\[BPZ,](#page-46-0) [MSei\]](#page-50-4), the mathematical structures of which are much richer than that of a UFC [\[S,](#page-51-4) [MSei,](#page-50-4) [LR,](#page-50-5) [Hu1\]](#page-48-2). It seems that a categorical description of a gapless edge as simple as that of a gapped edge is impossible.

Nevertheless, in the last 30 years, the mathematical theory of boundary-bulk (or open-closed) CFT's has been successfully developed at least in three different approaches:

- 1. the conformal-net approach (see [\[LR,](#page-50-5) [R1,](#page-50-6) [R2,](#page-51-5) [KLM\]](#page-49-3) and references therein),
- 2. the 2+1D-TQFT approach (see [\[FFFS,](#page-47-2) [FS1,](#page-47-3) [FRS1,](#page-47-4) [FjFRS2\]](#page-47-5) and references therein),
- 3. the vertex-operator-algebra approach (see [\[Hu5,](#page-48-3) [HK1,](#page-48-4) [HK2,](#page-48-5) [Ko4\]](#page-49-4) and references therein).

These mathematical developments have revealed a universal phenomenon: the mathematical structures of a boundary-bulk CFT can be split into two parts.

- 1. One part consists of a chiral algebra *V* (or a conformal net), also called a vertex operator algebra (VOA) in mathematics (see for example [\[LL\]](#page-50-7)), such that the category Mod*<sup>V</sup>* of *V*-modules is a modular tensor category [\[Hu7\]](#page-48-6).
- 2. The other parts are purely categorical structures, including certain algebras in Mod*<sup>V</sup>* and an algebra in the Drinfeld center Z(Mod*V*) of Mod*<sup>V</sup>* (see Theorem [4.7](#page-21-0) and [4.21\)](#page-25-1).

This suggests that it might be possible to describe a chiral gapless edge of a 2d topological order (C, *c*) by a pair (*V*, X ), where *V* is a VOA and X is a purely categorical structure that can be constructed from Mod*V*, C and perhaps some additional categorical data. The main goal of this paper is to show that this is indeed possible. More precisely, the main result of this work, summarized in Theoremph [6.7,](#page-38-0) says that X ] is an Mod*V*-enriched unitary fusion category (Definition [6.4\)](#page-36-0) satisfying some additional properties.

We explain the layout of this paper. In Section [2,](#page-4-0) we briefly review the mathematical theory of 2d topological orders and that of gapped edges. We emphasize previously overlooked details, such as the so-called spatial fusion anomalies, so that it makes our study of chiral gapless edge looks like a natural continuation. In Section [3,](#page-8-0) we carefully describe all possible observables (at a RG fixed point) on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge of a 2d topological order (C, *c*). In particular, we show that the observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge include a family of topological edge excitations, 0+1D boundary CFT's [\[C1,](#page-46-1) [C2,](#page-46-2) [CL\]](#page-46-3), 0D domain walls between boundary CFT's [\[FrFRS2\]](#page-47-6) and two kinds of fusions among domain walls. These boundary CFT's and domain walls are required to preserve a chiral symmetry given by a VOA *V* such that Mod*<sup>V</sup>* is assumed to be a UMTC. This symmetry condition allows us to describe all boundary CFT's, domain walls and their fusions as objects or morphisms in Mod*V*. As a consequence, all observables organize themselves into a single categorical structure X called an Mod*V*-enriched monoidal category. Therefore, a chiral gapless edge can be described by a pair (*V*, X ] ).

In order to further explore the additional hidden structures in X ] (in Section [6\)](#page-32-0), we need nearly all important mathematical results of rational CFT's in last 30 years. These results are unknown to most working physicists especially to those in the field of condensed matter physics. A briefly review of these results is necessary. In Section [4,](#page-18-0) we review the mathematical theory of boundary-bulk rational CFT's in the VOA approach [\[Hu5,](#page-48-3) [Z,](#page-51-6) [HL1,](#page-49-5) [LL,](#page-50-7) [Hu7,](#page-48-6) [Ko4\]](#page-49-4). This mathematical theory is not only a rigorous version of the physical theory but also a reformulation in terms of new and efficient categorical language, which, together with a classification result, play a crucial role in this work. In particular, in Section [4.1,](#page-19-0) we recall a Segal-type mathematical definition of a boundary-bulk CFT. In Section [4.2,](#page-20-0) we recall the classification theory of boundarybulk rational CFT's [\[LR,](#page-50-5) [R1,](#page-50-6) [R2,](#page-51-5) [FRS1,](#page-47-4) [FjFRS2,](#page-47-5) [Ko4,](#page-49-4) [KR2\]](#page-49-6). In Section [4.3,](#page-23-0) we explain the notion of an internal hom. In Section [4.4,](#page-25-0) we discuss issues related to unitary CFT's and show that boundary CFT's and domain walls among them can all be constructed from internal homs, and summarize all useful results in Theoremph [4.21.](#page-25-1)

After the preparation in Section [4,](#page-18-0) we are able to give a natural and explicit construction of chiral gapless edges in Section [5.1.](#page-27-0) It turns out that the enriched monoidal categories appearing there are only special cases of the so-called canonical construction (see Theorem [5.3\)](#page-28-0) [\[MP\]](#page-50-8). In Section [5.2,](#page-30-0) we will construct more general gapless edges by fusing the gapless edges constructed in Section [5.1](#page-27-0) with gapped domain walls, or equivalently, by the so-called *topological Wick rotations*. We will leave a loophole of our reasoning in Section [5.2](#page-30-0) and fix it in Section [6.3.](#page-40-0) Interestingly, these mathematical constructions automatically include all the gapped edges as special cases. Therefore, we obtain a unified mathematical theory of both gapped and chiral gapless edges.

In Section [6,](#page-32-0) we continue our exploration of the additional hidden structures in X . In particular, in Section [6.1,](#page-32-1) using the classification theory of boundary-bulk rational CFT's summarized in Theoremph [4.21,](#page-25-1) we reveal the hidden relations between Mod*<sup>V</sup>* and the underlying category X of X ] , and show that X ] is an Mod*V*-enriched unitary fusion category (see Definition [6.4\)](#page-36-0). In Section [6.2,](#page-37-0) using the boundary-bulk relation [\[KWZ2\]](#page-49-7) and the results in [\[KZ2\]](#page-50-9), we obtain a precise and complete mathematical description and a classification theory of chiral gapless edges of 2d topological orders. This is the main result of this paper, and is summarized in Theoremph [6.7.](#page-38-0) In the process of deriving the classification result, we will also discuss and propose a definition of a phase transition between two gapless phases via topological Wick rotations. In Section [6.3,](#page-40-0) motivated by a recurring phenomenon in this work, we propose a very general principle:

**Principle of Universality at RG fixed points**: A physical theory at a RG fixed point always satisfies a proper universal property in the mathematical sense.

We use it to fix the last loophole of our reasoning introduced in Section [5.2.](#page-30-0) This principle also provides a mathematical formulation of the spatial fusion anomaly.

In Section [7,](#page-42-0) we provide some outlooks for the study of gapless boundaries of higher dimensional topological orders. In particular, we will propose a surprising correspondence between gapped phases and gapless phases in all dimensions. In Appendix, we provide the mathematical definitions of some categorical notions in the enriched settings.

It is worthwhile to point out what is new in this paper. The main result Theoremph [6.7](#page-38-0) was first announced in [\[KZ3\]](#page-50-1) without providing any proofs. This paper contain many missing details and a complete proof. All physical (mathematically unrigorous) arguments used in the proof are explicitly spelled out. They are the No-Go Theorem (see Section [3.3\)](#page-12-0), Naturality Principle in physics (see Section [6.1\)](#page-32-1), the generalization of the mathematically rigorous Theorem [4.10](#page-22-0) to unitary cases (see Theoremph [4.21\)](#page-25-1), our definition of a purely edge phase transition (see Section [6.2\)](#page-37-0) and Principle of Universality at RG fixed points (see Section [6.3\)](#page-40-0). All the rest steps in the proof are mathematically rigorous. In this work, we also introduce a few brand new physical concepts for the first time, including spatial fusion anomaly, topological Wick rotation, a model-independent definition of a phase transition between gapless edges (see Section [6.2\)](#page-37-0), Principle of Universality and Gapped-Gapless Correspondence (see Section [7\)](#page-42-0).

In Part II [\[KZ4\]](#page-50-0), we will develop a mathematical theory of non-chiral gapless edges and 0d defects on a gapless edge. We will also give explicit calculations of various dimensional reduction processes and a complete mathematical description of boundary-bulk relation including both gapped and gapless edges. It is also worthwhile to mention that our theory of gapless edge provides a mathematical description of the critical points of topological phase transitions on the edges of 2d topological orders. An example was explained in [\[CJKYZ\]](#page-47-7).

## <span id="page-4-0"></span>2 Gapped defects of 2d topological orders

In this section, we review some of the basic facts of 2d topological orders, including the categorical description of its particle-like topological excitations, that of gapped edges and the boundary-bulk relation.

### <span id="page-4-1"></span>2.1 Particle-like topological excitations

An *n*d topological order is called *anomaly free* if it can be realized by an *n*d local Hamiltonian lattice model, and is called *anomalous* if otherwise [KW].

Before the discovery of fractional quantum Hall effect, a 2d gapped local Hamiltonian lattice model at zero temperature was viewed as "trivial" because all the correlation functions decay exponentially. It seems that there is no "local observables" in the long wave length limit. It was realized latter, however, the ground state degeneracy (GSD) of such 2d systems on surfaces with non-trivial topology, such as spheres with holes, torus, higher genus surfaces (with edges), etc., are non-trivial and different for different systems [TaW, NTW, NW]. It means that there are different kinds of "trivialness" of such "trivial" systems, which are, therefore, non-trivial.

Note that the GSD is a global observable [AKZ]. But the notion of a phase or a topological order is a local concept that is defined on an open disk (in the infinite system size limit). It was realized later that the local observables that are responsible for the non-trivial global observable GSD are the particle-like topological excitations (or anyons).

It is well known that all particle-like topological excitations form a mathematical structure called a unitary modular tensor category (UMTC) (see a review [Ki1, Appendix E]). The physical meanings of some (not all) ingredients of a UMTC  ${\mathfrak C}$  are precisely the observables in the long wave length limit, and are explained below.

- 1. An object in  $\mathcal{C}$  represents a particle-like topological excitation (or an anyon). The tensor unit  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}}$  represents the trivial topological excitation. A simple object represents a simple anyon, and a non-simple object, i.e. a direct sum of simple objects (e.g.  $\bigoplus_i x_i$  for simple  $x_i$ ), represents a composite anyon.
- 2. For any two anyons x and y, a morphism from x to y is an instanton, which is a localized defect on time axis (e.g. at  $t = t_0$ ), with two boundary conditions x (for  $t < t_0$ ) and y (for  $t > t_0$ ). If x and y are simple, then the space of instantons from the boundary condition x to y is given by  $hom_{\mathbb{C}}(x,y) = \delta_{x,y}\mathbb{C}$ . For two composite anyons  $\oplus_n x_n$  and  $\oplus_m y_m$ , where  $x_n, y_m$  are simple, we have

<span id="page-4-2"></span>
$$\text{hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(\oplus_n x_n, \oplus_m y_m) = \oplus_{n,m} \delta_{x_n,y_m} \mathbb{C}.$$

3. The fusion of instantons along the time axis, called a temporal fusion, defines a composition of morphisms

$$hom_{\mathcal{C}}(y,z) \times hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x,y) \to hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x,z)$$

$$(f,g) \mapsto f \circ g \tag{2.1}$$

Our convention is that the time coordinate of f is later than that of g. There is a distinguished morphism  $1_x \in \text{hom}_{\mathbb{C}}(x, x)$ , which is called the identity morphism, represents the trivial instanton. It is trivial in the sense that  $f \circ 1_x = f$  and  $1_y \circ f = f$  for  $f \in \text{hom}_{\mathbb{C}}(x, y)$ .

4. The tensor product functor  $\otimes$  :  $\mathcal{C} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}$  defined by  $(x, y) \mapsto x \otimes y$  describes the fusion between two anyons in spatial dimensions, called a spatial fusion. The trivial anyon is denoted by  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}}$ . We have  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}} \otimes x \simeq x \simeq x \otimes \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}}$ .

5. The spatial fusion  $\otimes$  of anyons also induces a spatial fusion of instantons. The naive guess of the space of instantons after the fusion is  $\hom_{\mathbb{C}}(x,y) \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \hom_{\mathbb{C}}(x',y')$ . But this naive guess is wrong because an anyon, viewed as a 0d topological order, is anomalous. The correct one is  $\hom_{\mathbb{C}}(x \otimes y, x' \otimes y')$ . In general, there is a map (induced from the universal property of  $\hom_{\mathbb{C}}(x \otimes y, x' \otimes y')$ )

$$\hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x, y) \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x', y') \xrightarrow{\otimes} \hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x \otimes y, x' \otimes y')$$
 (2.2)

for  $x, x', y, y' \in \mathbb{C}$ . Its failure of being an isomorphism is called *spatial fusion anomalies*. This is a recurring phenomenon in this work, and will be discussed in Section 6.3. It is also worthwhile to point out that the spatial fusion anomaly vanishes when  $x = y = x' = y' = 1_{\mathbb{C}}$ . This result has a non-trivial analogue for chiral gapless edges (see Remark 6.3).

6. The creation and annihilation of a particle-antiparticle pair are described by the duality morphisms

$$u_x: \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}} \to x \otimes x^*, \qquad v_x: x^* \otimes x \to \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}},$$
 (2.3)

where  $x^*$  is the anti-particle of x, satisfying some natural properties. Mathematically, this amounts to the rigidity of a UMTC.

7. Since the associated Hamiltonian model is gapped, one can adiabatically move one anyon around another one. This adiabatic movement defines a braiding isomorphism  $c_{x,y}$ :  $x \otimes y \to y \otimes x$  for  $x, y \in \mathbb{C}$ . The composed morphism  $x \otimes y \xrightarrow{c_{x,y}} y \otimes x \xrightarrow{c_{y,x}} x \otimes y$  is called a double braiding, which amounts to adiabatically moving anyon x around y (say clockwise) in a full circle.

The coherence conditions satisfied by the above data are all physically obvious. The non-degeneracy condition of the double braiding of a UMTC is stated as follows:

(\*) For a given simple anyon x, if its double braiding with all anyons y (including x itself) is trivial, i.e.  $(x \otimes y \xrightarrow{c_{x,y}} y \otimes x \xrightarrow{c_{y,x}} x \otimes y) = \mathrm{id}_{x \otimes y}$ , then x is the trivial anyon, i.e.  $x \simeq \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{C}}$ .

The condition  $(\star)$  is an anomaly-free condition, which says that all anyons in a topological order should be able to detect themselves via double braidings. If a system of anyons does not satisfy the condition  $(\star)$ , it must represent an anomalous 2d topological order, which must be a gapped boundary of a non-trivial 3+1D topological order [KW].

**Remark 2.1.** The space of instantons  $hom_{\mathcal{C}}(x, y)$  has another physical meaning. It is also the space of ground states of the topological order defined on a 2-sphere together with two anyons  $x^*$ , y. Two physical meanings of the same hom-space is a manifestation of the state-field correspondence of a TQFT or a topological order.

For each UMTC  $\mathcal{C}$ , one can define a topological central charge  $c_{\mathcal{C}}^{\text{top}}$ , which is defined modulo 8. We use  $\overline{\mathcal{C}}$  to denote the same monoidal category as  $\mathcal{C}$  but equipped with the braidings given by the anti-braidings in  $\mathcal{C}$ .  $\overline{\mathcal{C}}$  is also a UMTC. It was known that the Drinfeld center  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{C})$  of  $\mathcal{C}$  is given by  $\mathcal{C} \boxtimes \overline{\mathcal{C}}$  [Mu], where  $\boxtimes$  is the Deligne tensor product. The simplest UMTC is the category of finite dimensional Hilbert spaces, denoted by  $\mathbf{H}$ .

In the long wave length limit, are there any other physical observables in a 2d topological order? It turns out that there is a special state called the  $E_8$ -state (see for example [Ki2]), which is a non-trivial topological order but has no non-trivial anyon. It was known that its 1d edge is gapless and is given by the  $E_8$  chiral conformal field theory of central charge c = 8. It was generally accepted that a 2d topological order is completely determined by its anyons up to the  $E_8$ -states (i.e.  $E_8^{\boxtimes N}$ ) (see a review [W4]). We summarize this well-known result below.

Figure 1: This picture depicts a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{C},0)$ , where  $\mathcal{C}$  is a UMTC, with three gapped edges given by UFC's  $\mathcal{L},\mathcal{M},\mathcal{N}$ . The 2d bulk is oriented as the usual  $\mathbb{R}^2$  with the normal direction pointing out of the paper in readers' direction. The arrows indicate the induced orientation on the edge.

<span id="page-6-1"></span>A 2d (anomaly-free) topological order can be described mathematically by a pair  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ , where  $\mathcal{C}$  is a UMTC and c is the chiral central charge such that  $c_{\mathcal{C}}^{\text{top}} = c \pmod{8}$ .

The pair (H, 0) describes the trivial 2d topological order.

### <span id="page-6-0"></span>2.2 1d gapped edges

A 2d topological order ( $\mathcal{C}$ , 0) admitting a gapped edge is called *a non-chiral 2d topological order*. As depicted in Figure 1, the bulk phase ( $\mathcal{C}$ , 0) might have several different gapped edges, each of which represents a 1d anomalous topological order. Such an anomalous 1d topological order can be described mathematically by a unitary fusion category (UFC)  $\mathcal{L}$ , in which

- 1. an object represents a particle-like topological edge excitation;
- 2. for topological edge excitations  $x, y \in \mathcal{L}$ , the space of morphisms  $hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x, y)$  is the space of instantons (or localized defects on time axis) with boundary conditions x and y;
- 3. the composition of two morphisms describes the fusion of two instantons along the time axis;
- 4. the fusion product  $\otimes$  in  $\mathcal{L}$  represents the fusion of two such edge excitations in the spatial dimension according to the orientation of the edge.  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{L}}$  denote the trivial topological edge excitation, i.e.  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{L}} \otimes x \simeq x \simeq x \otimes \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{L}}$ .
- 5. This fusion also induces a fusion of two instantons in the spatial dimension. Again, the naive guess  $\hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x,y) \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x',y')$  for the space of instantons after the fusion is wrong. The correct one is  $\hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x \otimes x', y \otimes y')$ . In general, there is a map

<span id="page-6-2"></span>
$$\hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x,y) \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x',y') \xrightarrow{\otimes} \hom_{\mathcal{L}}(x \otimes x', y \otimes y'), \tag{2.4}$$

for  $x, x', y, y' \in \mathcal{L}$ . This map is not an isomorphism in general. Its failure of being an isomorphism is an indication of spatial fusion anomaly. This phenomenon is a recurring theme of this work. We will further explore this phenomenon as a special case of a more general principle in Section 6.3.

Note that there is no braiding because these topological excitations are restricted on the edge, thus cannot be braided.

The relation between the boundary phase and the bulk phase, or simply boundary-bulk relation, was known [KK, Ko6].

**Boundary-bulk relation**: The 2d bulk phase is uniquely determined by the anomalous 1d topological order on its boundary (or edge), and the UMTC  $\mathcal{C}$  describing the 2d bulk phase is given by the Drinfeld center of the UFC  $\mathcal{L}$  (or  $\mathcal{M}$ ,  $\mathcal{N}$ ) describing a 1d edge (as depicted in Figure 1), i.e.  $\mathcal{C} \simeq \mathcal{J}(\mathcal{L}) \simeq \mathcal{J}(\mathcal{M}) \simeq \mathcal{J}(\mathcal{N})$ .

Mathematically, different gapped edges  $\mathcal{L}, \mathcal{M}, \mathcal{N}$  that share the same bulk or Drinfeld center if and only if they are Morita equivalent as UFC's [Mu, ENO].

Importantly, although a UFC  $\mathcal{L}$  determines an anomalous 1d topological order, it does not fix a gapped edge of a 2d topological order completely. The same UFC might be realized as different edges of the same bulk. For example, the  $\mathbb{Z}_2$  2d topological order has two different gapped edges [BK], both of which realize the same 1d anomalous topological order defined by the UFC Rep( $\mathbb{Z}_2$ ).

The additional physical data that is needed to uniquely determine the edge is the information of how anyons in the bulk can be fused onto the edge. This information is given by a monoidal functor  $L: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{L}$  which factors through the forgetful functor  $f: \mathfrak{J}(\mathcal{L}) \to \mathcal{L}$ , i.e. there exists a braided monoidal functor  $L': \mathcal{C} \to \mathfrak{J}(\mathcal{L})$  such that the following diagram

<span id="page-7-1"></span>
$$\begin{array}{c}
C \xrightarrow{L'} 3(\mathcal{L}) \\
\downarrow^{\mathbf{f}} \\
\mathcal{L}
\end{array}$$
(2.5)

is commutative. Such a functor *L* is called a central functor [DMNO].

**Remark 2.2.** The mathematical theory of 0d domain walls between different gapped edges is also known [KK, Ko6, AKZ].

### <span id="page-7-0"></span>2.3 What happens to gapless edges?

We have seen that we have a beautiful mathematical theory of gapped edges of 2d topological orders. Ironically, experimentally discovered 2d topological orders, such as those discovered in quantum Hall effects, all have topologically protected gapless edges. The natural question is whether there is a similar mathematical theory for gapless edges. As depicted in Figure 2, this question contains at least the following three parts:

- 1. What is the mathematical description of a gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ ?
- 2. Does the boundary-bulk relation, i.e. bulk = the center of the edge, still hold?
- 3. What is the mathematical description of a 0d gapless domain wall between two gapless 1d edges?

In [KWZ2], using a very formal argument, we have shown that the bulk topological order (in any dimensions) should be given by the center of its boundary, i.e.

$$\mathfrak{C} \simeq \mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{L}) \simeq \mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{M}) \simeq \mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{N}).$$

regardless whether the boundary is gapped or gapless [KWZ2, Remark 5.7], and regardless what the mathematical description of a gapless boundary is.

**Remark 2.3.** A reader might be puzzled by the above statement. How could one know the notion of the center of certain algebraic object before we have a complete mathematical definition of the algebraic object. This is because the notion of center is defined by its universal property, which only depends on the notion of a morphism between two such algebraic objects. In [KWZ2], the notion of a morphism between two anomalous nd topological orders was introduced without knowing how to define an nd topological order.

<span id="page-8-2"></span>Figure 2: Obvious questions about gapless edges of a 2d chiral topological order.

This result [\[KWZ2,](#page-49-7) Remark 5.7] convinced us that there should be a unified mathematical theory of gapped and gapless edges of 2d topological orders. We will answer the first question for chiral gapless edges in this work, and answers the second and third questions in [\[KZ4\]](#page-50-0).

**Remark 2.4.** The result of [\[KWZ2,](#page-49-7) Remark 5.7] and the success of this work have non-trivial and exciting predictions for the study of higher dimensional gapped and gapless phases. We will briefly discuss them in Section [7.](#page-42-0)

## <span id="page-8-0"></span>**3 Chiral gapless edge I**

In this section, we will try to find all observables living on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge of a 2+1D topological order, which is fixed to be (C, *c*).

## <span id="page-8-1"></span>**3.1 Vertex operator algebras**

Suppose that the 2d bulk phase (C, *c*) is realized on an open 2-disk as depicted in Figure [3.](#page-9-0) We choose a complex coordinate *z* on the 1+1D world sheet of the chiral gapless edge (i.e. the 2D boundary of the solid cylinder) such that the time axis is the real axis.

We assume that gapless edge modes are completely chiral. It is known that the chiral edge modes are states in a chiral conformal field theory with the central charge *c* (see [\[Ha,](#page-48-1)[W1,](#page-51-1)[MR,](#page-50-2)[W2\]](#page-51-2), reviews in [\[W2,](#page-51-2) [W3,](#page-51-3) [NSSFS\]](#page-50-3) and references therein). This chiral CFT is defined on the 1+1D world sheet of the edge. We denote this chiral CFT by *U*. By the state-field correspondence in a 2D CFT, there is a bijective map *Y* from *U* to the space of chiral fields, still denoted by *U*:

$$Y: \phi \mapsto Y(\phi, z) = \phi(z).$$

The notation φ(*z*) is commonly used in physics literature and *Y*(φ, *z*) is commonly used in mathematics literature. We prefer to use the mathematical notation *Y*(φ, *z*) sometimes in this work because the first notion can be quite ambiguous whenever we discuss intertwining operators. We should view these chiral fields as "local observables" on the world sheet. Since these chiral fields can live on the entire 1+1D world sheet, they cannot be multi-valued. Therefore, these chiral fields, say φ(*z*), contains only integer powers of the complex variable *z*, i.e.

$$\phi(z) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \phi_n z^{-n-1},$$

whereφ*<sup>n</sup>* ∈ End(*U*). Any two chiral fields in *U* can have the so-called operator product expansion (OPE) as shown below:

$$\phi(z_1)\psi(z_2) \sim \frac{(\psi_k \phi)(z_2)}{(z_1 - z_2)^{k+1}} + \frac{(\psi_{k-1} \phi)(z_2)}{(z_1 - z_2)^k} + \cdots, \quad \text{for some } k \in \mathbb{N}.$$

Figure 3: This picture depicts a 2d topological order (C, *c*) on a 2-disk, together with a 1d gapless edge, propagating in time. A chiral field φ(*z*) ∈ *U* is depicted on the 1+1D world sheet.

<span id="page-9-0"></span>This OPE is commutative, i.e. φ(*z*1)ψ(*z*2) ∼ ψ(*z*2)φ(*z*1). It provides *U* with an algebraic structure, which is called a chiral algebra in physics, and is rigorously defined in mathematics under a different name: a vertex operator algebra (VOA) (see for example [\[LL\]](#page-50-7) for a review). In this work, we use the following working definition of a VOA, which is sometimes called a CFT-type VOA. We avoid to use formal variables that are commonly used in mathematics literature.

**Definition 3.1.** A *vertex operator algebra* (VOA) *U* consists of the following data:

- a **C**-linear vector space *U* graded by non-negative integers (called conformal weights), i.e. *U* = ⊕*n*∈**N***U*(*n*) ,
- a distinguished element **1** ∈ *U*(0) called vacuum state,
- a map *Y* : (*U* ⊗**<sup>C</sup>** *U*) × **C** <sup>×</sup> → *U* := Q *<sup>n</sup>*∈**<sup>N</sup>** *U*(*n*) called vertex operator

$$(u \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} v, z) \mapsto Y(u, z)v = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} u_n v \, z^{-n-1},$$

• a distinguished element ω*<sup>U</sup>* ∈ *U*(2) called Virasoro element,

satisfying the following axioms:

- 1. dim *U*(*n*) < ∞ and *U*(0) = **C1**;
- 2. if *u* ∈ *U*(*k*) , then *u<sup>n</sup>* maps *U*(*m*) into *U*(*m*+*k*−*n*−1);
- 3. *Y*(**1**, *z*) = id*<sup>U</sup>* and *u*<sup>−</sup>1**1** = *u* for *u* ∈ *U*;
- 4. for *u*, *v*, *w* ∈ *U* and *w* <sup>0</sup> ∈ *U*<sup>0</sup> := ⊕*nU*<sup>∗</sup> (*n*) , the following three expressions

$$\langle w', Y(u, z_1)Y(v, z_2)w \rangle$$
,  $\langle w', Y(v, z_2)Y(u, z_1)w \rangle$ ,  $\langle w', Y(Y(u, z_1 - z_2)v, z_2)w \rangle$ 

converge absolutely in three different domains |*z*1| > |*z*2| > 0, |*z*2| > |*z*1| > 0, |*z*2| > |*z*1−*z*2| > 0, respectively, to the same rational function *f*(*z*1, *z*2) (with poles only at *z*1, *z*<sup>2</sup> = 0, ∞ and *z*<sup>1</sup> = *z*2);

5. we have *Y*(ω*U*, *z*) = P *<sup>n</sup>*∈**<sup>Z</sup>** *L*(*n*)*z* −*n*−2 such that *L*(*n*), *n* ∈ **Z** generate the famous Virasoro algebra, i.e.

$$[L(m), L(n)] = (m-n)L(m+n) + \frac{c}{12}(m^3 - m)\delta_{m+n,0}$$

where the number *c* is called the central charge;

Figure 4: This picture depicts a 2d topological order (C, *c*) on a 2-disk, together with a 1d gapless edge, propagating in time. At *t* = 0, we move a bulk particle *a* to the edge. The blue world line is supported on *x*.

- <span id="page-10-1"></span>6. for *u* ∈ *U*(*n*) , we have *L*(0)*u* = *nu*;
- 7. for *u* ∈ *U*, we have *Y*(*L*(−1)*u*, *z*) = *d dzY*(*u*, *z*).

**Remark 3.2.** *Y*(ω*U*, *z*) is called the energy momentum tensor in physics. It generates a sub-VOA hω*U*i, which is the smallest sub-VOA of *U*. The number *c* is the same central charge as the one in the 2d bulk phase (C, *c*).

### <span id="page-10-0"></span>**3.2 Open-string vertex operator algebras**

Is this VOA *U* the only observables on the 1+1D world sheet of the edge? Consider the situation depicted in Figure [4.](#page-10-1) At *t* = 0, a topological excitation *a* in the bulk is moved to the edge. It becomes a topological edge excitation *x*. As a typical quantum quenching scenario, this movement suddenly changes the microscopic physics at the location of *x*. As a consequence, the RG flow will drive the world line supported on *x* to a fixed point theory, which is denoted by *Ax*. In particular, *A<sup>x</sup>* should contain all the physical observables on the world line at the RG fixed point. It is clear that when *a* = **1**C, *x* is the trivial topological edge excitations, denoted by **1**. Therefore, we must have *A***<sup>1</sup>** = *U*. More general topological edge excitations can be created by inserting impurities from outside. In other words, there are potentially more topological edge excitations than those obtained from moving bulk excitations to the edge.

At the RG fixed point, the conformal symmetry is restored. The observables in *A<sup>x</sup>* are again chiral fields that can have OPE along the world line. But the chiral fields in *A<sup>x</sup>* are potentially different from those in *U*. By our choice of complex coordinate, the world line (or the time axis) is the real axis. There is no multi-value issue here because the chiral fields are restricted on the real axis. As a consequence, the chiral fields on the world line can have non-integer powers, i.e.

$$\phi(r) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{R}} \phi_n r^{-n-1},$$

Moreover, two chiral fields on the world line can also have OPE as follows:

$$\psi(r_1)\phi(r_2) \sim \frac{(\psi_k\phi)(r_2)}{(r_1-r_2)^{k+1}} + \frac{(\psi_{k-1}\phi)(r_2)}{(r_1-r_2)^k} + \cdots$$
, for some  $k \in \mathbb{R}$ .

This OPE is potentially non-commutative, i.e.

$$\psi(r_1)\phi(r_2) \nsim \phi(r_2)\psi(r_1).$$

More precisely, OPE among all these chiral fields defines an algebraic structure on *A<sup>x</sup>* called an open-string vertex operator algebra (OSVOA) [\[HK1\]](#page-48-4). We recall this notion below.

**Definition 3.3.** An *open-string vertex operator algebra* (OSVOA) *A* of central charge *c* consisting of the following data:

- an  $\mathbb{R}$ -graded vector space  $A = \bigoplus_{n \in \mathbb{R}} A_{(n)}$ ,
- a map  $Y_A: (A \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} A) \times \mathbb{R}_+ \to \overline{A} := \prod_{n \in \mathbb{R}} A_{(n)}$  defined by  $(u \otimes v, r) \mapsto Y_A(u, r)v$ ,
- two distinguished elements  $\mathbb{1} \in A_{(0)}$  and  $\omega_A \in A_{(2)}$ ,

satisfying the following conditions:

- 1. dim  $A_{(n)} < \infty$  and  $A_{(n)} = 0$  for  $n \ll 0$ ;
- 2.  $Y(1, r) = id_A$  and  $u_{-1}1 = u$  for  $u \in A$ ;
- 3. for  $u_1, \dots, u_n, w \in A$  and  $w' \in A' = \bigoplus_n A^*_{(n)}$ , the series:

$$\langle w', Y_A(u_1, r_1) \cdots Y_A(u_n, r_n) w \rangle := \sum_{m_1, \cdots, m_{n-1}} \langle w', Y_A(u_1, r_1) P_{m_1} \cdots P_{m_{n-1}} Y_A(u_n, r_n) w \rangle,$$

where  $P_k : \overline{A} \twoheadrightarrow A_{(k)}$  is the projection operator, converges absolutely when  $r_1 > \cdots > r_n > 0$  and can be extended to a potentially multi-valued complex analytic function in  $(\mathbb{C}^{\times})^n$  with only possible singularities at  $z_i = z_j$  for  $i, j = 1, \cdots, n$  and  $i \neq j$ ;

4. the following two expressions

$$\langle w', Y_A(u_1, r_1)Y_A(u_2, r_2)w \rangle$$
,  $\langle w', Y_A(Y_A(u, r_1 - r_2)v, r_2)w \rangle$ 

converge absolutely in two different domains  $r_1 > r_2 > 0$  and  $r_2 > r_1 - r_2 > 0$ , respectively, and equal on the intersection of the above two domains;

- 5. we have  $Y_A(\omega_A, r) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} L(n) r^{-n-2}$  such that  $\{L(n)\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$  generate the Virasoro algebra of central charge c;
- 6. for  $u \in A_{(n)}$ , we have L(0)u = nu;
- 7. for  $u \in A$ , we have  $Y_A(L(-1)u, r) = \frac{d}{dr}Y(u, r)$ .

An OSVOA is a non-commutative generalization of the notion of a VOA. In particular, a VOA is automatically an OSVOA. An OSVOA contains a smallest subalgebra  $\langle \omega_A \rangle \subset A$  generated by  $\omega_A$ . Actually,  $\langle \omega_A \rangle$  is a VOA. More generally, the following subspace of an OSVOA A:

$$C_0(A) = \{ u \in \bigoplus_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} A_{(n)} | Y_A(u, r) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} u_n r^{-n-1}, \quad Y_A(v, r) u = e^{rL(-1)} Y(u, -r) v, \forall v \in A \},$$
 (3.1)

defines a subalgebra of A called the *meromorphic center* of A. Moreover, it is a VOA. The defining property of  $C_0(A)$  is equivalent to the following condition:

(•) For  $u \in C_0(A)$ ,  $v, w \in A$  and  $w' \in A'$ , there exists a (possibly multi-valued) analytic function on

$$\{(z_1, z_2) \in \mathbb{C}^2 | z_1 \neq 0, z_2 \neq 0, z_1 \neq z_2 \}.$$

such that it is single valued in  $z_1$  and equals to

$$\langle w', Y_A(u, z_1)Y_A(v, r_2)w \rangle$$
,  $\langle w', Y_A(v, r_2)Y_A(v, z_1)w \rangle$ ,  $\langle w', Y_A(Y_A(u, z_1 - r_2)v, r_2)w \rangle$ 

in the domains  $|z_1| > r_2 > 0$ ,  $r_2 > |z_1| > 0$ ,  $r_2 > |z_1 - r_2| > 0$ , respectively;

Figure 5: The picture (a) depicts a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  on a 2-disk, together with a 1d gapless edge, propagating in time. At t = 0, a topological edge excitation x is created. At  $t = t_1 > 0$ , the edge excitation is changed to y. This change creates a domain wall  $M_{x,y}$  between OSVOA's  $A_x$  and  $A_y$ . <span id="page-12-1"></span>It is clear that A is a  $C_0(A)$ -module. For  $v \in A$ , it can be shown that there exist  $v_n$  such that  $Y_A(v,r) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{R}} v_n r^{-n-1}$  [HK1, Prop. 1.4]. We introduce a formal vertex operator:

$$Y_A^f(v,x) := \sum_{n \in \mathbb{R}} v_n x^{-n-1},$$

where x is a formal variable.

<span id="page-12-2"></span>**Theorem 3.4** ([HK1]). The condition (•) is equivalent to the statement that the formal vertex operator  $Y_A^f(-,x)$  is an intertwining operator of  $C_0(A)$ .

## <span id="page-12-0"></span>3.3 Boundary CFT's and domain walls

More general situations can occur on the same world line as depicted in Figure 5 (a). More precisely, one can insert an impurity at  $t = t_1 > 0$  on the world line so that the topological edge excitation is changed from x to y. As a consequence, the OSVOA living on the  $\{t > t_1\}$ -part of the world line is given by  $A_y$ . The domain wall between  $A_x$  and  $A_y$  also contains chiral fields (sometimes called defect fields in CFT's). This domain wall is similar to the instantons on a gapped edge (recall Section 2.2). We denote the space of such defect fields by  $M_{x,y}$ . Then it is clear that the space of defect fields localized around x is  $M_{1,x}$ . Moreover, we should have  $M_{x,x} = A_x$  and  $M_{1,1} = A_1 = U$ . This space  $M_{x,y}$  should also be an  $\mathbb{R}$ -graded vector space, i.e.  $M_{x,y} = \bigoplus_{n \in \mathbb{R}} (M_{x,y})_{(n)}$ , such that  $\dim(M_{x,y})_{(n)} < \infty$ .

Similar to the fusion of instantons along the time axis in the gapped edge case, defect fields in different domain walls can also have OPE along the world line. This type of OPE defines a vertex operator:

<span id="page-12-3"></span>
$$Y_{(z,y,x)}: (M_{y,z} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} M_{x,y}) \times \mathbb{R}_+ \to \overline{M}_{x,z}, \qquad (u \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} v,r) \mapsto Y_{(z,y,x)}(u,r)v. \tag{3.2}$$

This OPE should also be associative. More precisely, it satisfies the following condition.

• Associativity of OPE: for  $u \in M_{y,z}$ ,  $v \in M_{x,y}$ ,  $w \in M_{w,x}$  and  $w' \in M'_{w,z}$ , the following two expressions

$$\langle w', Y_{(z,y,w)}(u,r_1)Y_{(y,x,w)}(v,r_2)w \rangle$$
,  $\langle w', Y_{(z,x,w)}(Y_{(z,y,x)}(u,r_1-r_2)v,r_2)w \rangle$ 

converge absolutely in the domains: *r*<sup>1</sup> > *r*<sup>2</sup> > 0 and *r*<sup>2</sup> > *r*<sup>1</sup> − *r*<sup>2</sup> > 0, respectively, and coincide in the domain *r*<sup>1</sup> > *r*<sup>2</sup> > *r*<sup>1</sup> − *r*<sup>2</sup> > 0.

It is important to note that we have not yet discussed the issue if these *Mx*,*<sup>y</sup>* or *A<sup>x</sup>* are really different from *U*. At least, it is clear that there should be some relations among *U*, *A<sup>x</sup>* and *Mx*,*y*. For example, *A<sup>x</sup>* acts on *Mx*,*<sup>y</sup>* from one side and *A<sup>y</sup>* acts from the other side, and *U* acts on *Mx*,*<sup>y</sup>* from above (or below) the time axis, and *U* can be mapped into *Ax*, etc. In order to formulate them precisely, we need first to show that these *A<sup>x</sup>* are more than just OSVOA's. They are actually boundary CFT's [\[C1,](#page-46-1) [C2,](#page-46-2) [CL\]](#page-46-3) that share the same bulk 1+1D CFT. Indeed, consider a dimensional reduction process, which starts from Figure [5](#page-12-1) (a), and gradually squeeze the solid cylinder, and ends up in the 1+1D world line depicted in Figure [5](#page-12-1) (b). The 0+1D boundary of this 1+1D world sheet is precisely given by the world line supported on *x*. Observables on this world line remain the same during this process. It is clear that both chiral and anti-chiral fields live on the resulting 1+1D world sheet. It was shown in [\[CZ,](#page-46-6) Sec. 2.1] that they form a modular invariant bulk CFT *A*bulk. This fact was emphasized in [\[RZ,](#page-51-9) [Le\]](#page-50-13) as a consequence of the following "no-go theorem": *Any 1*+*1D CFT's realized by 1d lattice Hamiltonian models should be modular invariant*. Therefore, the OSVOA's *A<sup>x</sup>* and *A<sup>y</sup>* must be the boundary CFT's sharing the same modular invariant bulk CFT *A*bulk, and *Mx*,*<sup>y</sup>* is a domain wall between them and is also compatible with the same bulk. Mathematically, however, it is not a clear statement because there are different mathematical definitions of a boundary-bulk CFT. Here we prefer to make this statement mathematically precise. We propose a stronger "no-go theorem".

**No-go Theorem**: A 1+1D boundary-bulk CFT realized by a 1d lattice Hamiltonian model with boundaries should satisfy all the axioms in the mathematical definition of a boundary-bulk CFT given in Definition [4.1](#page-19-1) or [4.4.](#page-20-1)

In particular, the boundary-bulk CFT should satisfy all the consistence conditions, including the famous modular invariant condition [\[BPZ,](#page-46-0) [MSei\]](#page-50-4) and Cardy condition [\[C2,](#page-46-2) [CL\]](#page-46-3).

**Remark 3.5.** Using boundary CFT's to study 0d defects or impurities in other condensed matter systems has a long history [\[AL1,](#page-46-7) [AL2\]](#page-46-8). But we are not aware of any earlier works mentioning the appearance of the boundary CFT's on the gapless edges of 2d topological orders. That a chiral vertex operator living in *M***1**,*<sup>x</sup>* was known in 90's (see [\[WW,](#page-51-10) [WWH\]](#page-51-11)).

**Remark 3.6.** Once we established the precise mathematical description of a chiral gapless edge, we can compute this dimensional reduction process and identify precisely which modular invariant bulk CFT is obtained at the end of this process [\[KZ3\]](#page-50-1) (see more details in [\[KZ4\]](#page-50-0)).

In summary, observables on the 1+1D world sheet of the edge of a 2d topological order form a "not-yet-categorical" structure:

- 1. objects are topological edge excitations: *x*, *y*, · · · ;
- 2. the space of morphisms are *Mx*,*<sup>y</sup>* (boundary CFT's and domain walls);
- 3. there is a map given by the associative OPE *Y*(*z*,*y*,*x*) : (*My*,*<sup>z</sup>* ⊗**<sup>C</sup>** *Mx*,*y*) × **R**<sup>+</sup> → *Mx*,*z*.

We can not yet claim it is a categorical structure not only because the OPE is not yet a composition map but also because the "identity morphisms" are missing. We will discuss this important missing data in the next subsection.

### <span id="page-13-0"></span>**3.4 Chiral symmetries**

The no-go theorem demands *A<sup>x</sup>* to be a boundary CFT, which requires many additional structures beyond that of an OSVOA. One of them is called the boundary condition of a boundary CFT. It says that, as a boundary CFT,  $A_x$  should satisfy a conformal invariant boundary condition (as the minimal requirement) or a V-invariant boundary condition in general, where V is called the chiral symmetry. We explain this in detail in this subsection.

Consider moving chiral fields in U into those in  $A_x$  along a path  $\gamma$  (e.g.  $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$  in Figure 6). This process defines a linear map  $\iota_{\gamma}: U \to A_x$ , which is clearly independent of the homotopy type of the path. Therefore, as shown in Figure 6, there are essentially two independent ways of mapping U into  $A_x$  along the paths  $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ , respectively, i.e.  $\iota_{\gamma_1}, \iota_{\gamma_2}: U \to A_x$ . It is clear that the vacuum state and the OPE of the chiral fields must be preserved in these processes. Therefore,  $\iota_{\gamma_1}, \iota_{\gamma_2}$  are two OSVOA homomorphisms. The minimal requirement for  $A_x$  to give a consistent boundary CFT is that  $\iota_{\gamma}$  should satisfy the following condition:

- Conformal-invariant boundary condition [C1, Ko3]:
  - 1.  $\iota_{\gamma_1}|_{\langle\omega_U\rangle}=\iota_{\gamma_2}|_{\langle\omega_U\rangle}$ ;
  - 2.  $\iota_{\gamma_1}|_{\langle \omega_{U} \rangle} : \langle \omega_{U} \rangle \to \langle \omega_{A_x} \rangle$  defines an isomorphism of VOA.

Let *V* be a sub-VOA of *U*, i.e.  $\langle \omega_U \rangle \subset V \subset U$ . In most general situation, we expect the following condition to be true.

- *V-invariant boundary condition* [Ko3, Definition 1.25]:
  - 1.  $\iota_{\gamma_1}|_V = \iota_{\gamma_2}|_V$ ;
  - 2.  $\iota_{v_1}|_V:V\to A_x$  is injective.

We denote that path independent map by  $\iota_x : V \hookrightarrow A_x$ 

In other words, chiral fields in V can move transparently on the entirely 1+1D world (except those 0D walls  $M_{x,y}$  for  $x \neq y$ ). If the V-invariant boundary condition holds for all  $A_x$ , then, for a sub-VOA V' of V, the V'-invariant boundary condition holds automatically. If we require the set of topological edge excitations to be fixed, then there will be the maximal sub-VOA V in U that  $A_x$  is V-invariant for all x. This VOA V will be called the *chiral symmetry* of the edge. From now on, we take V to be the chiral symmetry of the edge.

<span id="page-14-0"></span>**Remark 3.7.** If we demands  $V' \subseteq V$  to be the chiral symmetry, then the set of topological edge excitations can be enlarged accordingly. We will discuss this issue in Section 6.2.

**Remark 3.8.** It can happen that the chiral symmetry is maximal, i.e V = U, as we will show in Section 5.1. We will also show in Section 5.2 that  $\langle \omega_U \rangle \subsetneq V \subsetneq U$  in general.

By the *V*-invariant boundary condition,  $M_{x,y}$  are all *V*-modules in the usual sense [FHL]. We denote the category of *V*-modules by Mod<sub>*V*</sub>. We obtain the following property of  $Y_{A_x}$ :

• for  $\phi \in V$ ,  $v, w \in A_x$  and  $w' \in A'_x$ , the operator products

$$\langle w', \phi(z) Y_{A_{\nu}}(v,t) w \rangle$$

## <span id="page-18-0"></span>4 Boundary-bulk CFT's

Further study of  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$  needs the classification theory of rational CFT's (see Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 4.21 and Remark 4.26). In this section, we review the mathematical theory of rational CFT's based on the representation theory of VOA's.

#### <span id="page-19-0"></span>4.1 Definitions of boundary-bulk CFT's

The theory of conformal field theory (CFT) was developed by physicists at the end of 80's (see [BPZ, MSei] and [FMS] for a lengthy review). It took, however, about 20 years for mathematicians to develop a successful mathematical foundation of CFT's. This foundation is not merely a rigorization of the existing physical theory in its old formalism, but a new mathematical theory written in a beautiful new language with powerful new tools, and it leads to many new results far beyond those in [FMS]. These new results play a crucial role in this work, and are quite unfamiliar to most condensed matter physicists. Therefore, we briefly outline here where these results come from. See [Ko5] for a more detailed review.

When physicists Belavin, Polyakov and Zamolodchikov published the first systematic study of 2D CFT's in 1984 [BPZ], mathematicians Frenkel, Lepowsky and Meurman published their independent discovery of similar mathematical structures also in 1984 [FLM]. In 1987, the preprint of Segal's mathematical definition of a 2D CFT [S] came out and have made a big impact to mathematics community since then. We will give a sketchy presentation of an open-closed (or boundary-bulk) generalization of Segal's definition [Hu5, HKr, Ko5].

We define  $\mathbf{Bord}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}^{\mathrm{op}}$  to be the category, in which (1) the objects are finite ordered set with two colors: "c" (for closed strings) and "o" (for open strings), e.g.  $\{o_1, c_2, o_3, c_4, c_5\}$ ; (2) the spaces of morphisms are those of the conformal equivalence classes of open-closed complex bordisms, i.e. the moduli spaces of Riemann surfaces with boundary components being either completely parametrized or not parametrized but having none or some parametrized line segments, e.g.

where the label "in/out" is associated to domain/codomain of the morphism and is determined by the relation between the orientation of the boundary component induced from that of the surface and that induced from the parametrization.

The category **Bord**<sup>cpx</sup><sub>op-cl</sub> has the structure of a symmetric monoidal category with the tensor product defined by the disjoint union. Moreover, it has a \*-structure defined by flipping the orientation of the Riemann surfaces. This flipping exchanges the domains with the codomains. Let  $\mathbf{H}^{\infty}$  be the category of Hilbert spaces. It also has a \*-structure  $\operatorname{hom}_{\mathbf{H}^{\infty}}(x,y) \xrightarrow[z]{+} \operatorname{hom}_{\mathbf{H}^{\infty}}(y,x)$  defined by taking adjoint.

<span id="page-19-1"></span>**Definition 4.1** ([S, Hu5, HKr]). A boundary-bulk (or open-closed) CFT is a real-analytic projective symmetric monoidal functor from  $\mathbf{Bord}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}^{\mathrm{cpx}}$  to  $\mathbf{H}^{\infty}$ . It is called *unitary* if it is also a \*-functor.

**Remark 4.2.** Since the spaces of morphisms in  $Bord_{op-cl}^{cpx}$  are moduli spaces naturally equipped with complex structures, "real-analytic" means that the functor, restricting on the spaces of morphisms, gives real-analytic functions. If the functor is complex analytic, then such CFT's are called holomorphic CFT's.

**Remark 4.3.** A CFT functor F maps  $\{c_1\}$ , i.e. the set containing a single "c"-colored element, to  $H_{\text{bulk}}$ , and maps  $\{o_1\}$  to  $H_{\text{bdy}}$ . The monoidalness of F means that

$$F(\{o_1,c_2,o_3,c_4,c_5\}) \simeq H_{\mathrm{bdy}} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} H_{\mathrm{bulk}} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} H_{\mathrm{bdy}} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} H_{\mathrm{bulk}} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} H_{\mathrm{bulk}}.$$

This definition of CFT includes all consistence conditions, such as the modular invariant condition and the famous Cardy condition, etc. Unfortunately, this definition is not directly workable because chiral fields  $\phi(z)$  in a CFT are associated to insertion at a point instead of a boundary component (or a line segment). It suggests to consider Riemann surfaces with parametrized punctures in the interior (closed strings stretched to infinity) or on the boundaries (open strings stretched to infinity) [V, Hu1, Ko5]. We will denote this surface-with-puncture variation of  $\mathbf{Bord}^{\mathrm{cpx},\infty}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}$  by  $\mathbf{Bord}^{\mathrm{cpx,\infty}}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}$ . Actually,  $\mathbf{Bord}^{\mathrm{cpx,\infty}}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}$  is not a category because the composition of morphisms are only partially defined, thus is called a partial category. Similarly, we replace  $\mathbf{H}^{\infty}$  by a partial category of graded vector spaces (with a subtle definition of morphisms and their compositions), denoted by  $\mathbf{GV}$  [Hu1]. It is also possible to endow each of  $\mathbf{Bord}^{\mathrm{cpx,\infty}}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}$  and  $\mathbf{GV}$  with a \*-structures [Gui1]. Then we obtain a working definition of a CFT.

<span id="page-20-1"></span>**Definition 4.4** ([Ko5]). A *boundary-bulk* (*or an open-closed*) *CFT* is a real-analytic projective symmetric monoidal functor  $F : \mathbf{Bord}_{\mathrm{op-cl}}^{\mathrm{cpx},\infty} \to \mathbf{GV}$ . It is *unitary* if it is also a \*-functor.

To distinguish it from the first definition, we denote  $F(\{c_1\})$  by  $V_{\text{bulk}}$  and  $F(\{o_1\})$  by  $V_{\text{bdv}}$ .

### <span id="page-20-0"></span>4.2 Classification theory of boundary-bulk CFT's

If we restrict a CFT functor F to the partial subcategory consisting of only bordisms of genus zero (Riemann spheres) from  $\{c_1, \cdots, c_n\}_{n=0}^{\infty}$  to  $\{c_1\}$  and assume F is complex analytic, it was proved by Huang that this restricted F endows  $V_{\text{bulk}}$  with the structure of a vertex operator algebra (VOA) [Hu1]. Therefore, a VOA is a substructure of a CFT. In general, a VOA is not modular invariant except for holomorphic VOA's (such as the Monster Moonshine VOA [FLM]). The idea to find a modular invariant bulk (or closed) CFT  $V_{\text{bulk}}$  is to realize it as certain extension of  $V \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}$ , where V is a VOA and  $\overline{V}$  is the same as the VOA V in the formal variable but contains only the anti-chiral fields  $\phi(\overline{z}) = \sum_n \phi_n \overline{z}^{-n-1}$ . Therefore, we need study the representation theory of VOA. Building on many earlier works by Huang and Lepowsky on tensor category theory for VOA's [HL1, HL2, Hu2], Zhu's influential work on modular invariance [Z] and Huang's proof of Verlinde formula [Hu7], Huang proved that the category  $\mathrm{Mod}_V$  of V-modules for V satisfying certain rationality conditions is a modular tensor category [Hu8]. As a consequence, a bulk CFT  $V_{\mathrm{bulk}}$  is a commutative algebra in the category of  $V \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}$ -modules [Ko2], or equivalently, in the category  $\mathrm{Mod}_V \boxtimes \overline{\mathrm{Mod}_V}$ , which is also the Drinfeld center  $\Im(\mathrm{Mod}_V)$  of  $\mathrm{Mod}_V$ . Similarly, the boundary CFT  $V_{\mathrm{bdy}}$  is a chiral extension of a VOA.

Before we state the classification result, we need recall some basic notions in tensor categories.

<span id="page-20-2"></span>**Definition 4.5.** An *algebra* in a monoidal category A is a triple  $A = (A, m, \eta)$  where A is an object of A, m (the multiplication) is a morphism  $A \otimes A \to A$  and  $\eta$  (the unit) is a morphism  $\mathbf{1}_A \to A$  such that

$$m\circ (m\otimes 1_A)=m\circ (1_A\otimes m),\quad m\circ (1_A\otimes \eta)=1_A=m\circ (\eta\otimes 1_A).$$

An algebra A in a braided monoidal category is called *commutative* if  $m_A \circ c_{AA} = m_A$ .

Similarly, one can define a *coalgebra*  $A = (A, \Delta, \epsilon)$ , where  $\Delta : A \to A \otimes A$  and  $\epsilon : A \to \mathbf{1}_A$  obey the following coassociativity and counit conditions:

$$(\Delta \otimes 1_A) \circ \Delta = (1_A \otimes \Delta) \circ \Delta, \quad (\epsilon \otimes 1_A) \circ \Delta = 1_A = (1_A \otimes \epsilon) \circ \Delta.$$

**Definition 4.6.** A *Frobenius algebra*  $A = (A, m, \eta, \Delta, \epsilon)$  is both an algebra and a coalgebra such that the coproduct  $\Delta$  is an A-A-bimodules map, i.e.

$$(1_A \otimes m) \circ (\Delta \otimes 1_A) = \Delta \otimes m = (m \otimes 1_A) \circ (1_A \otimes \Delta).$$

We will use the following graphical representation:

$$m = \bigwedge_{A = A}^{A}$$

satisfying the modular invariant condition, for all *i*, *j* ∈ Irr(Mod*V*),

$$\iota \circ \iota^* = \bigotimes_{\otimes^R(A_{\text{bdy}})}^{\otimes^R(A_{\text{bdy}})} \tag{4.4}$$

**Definition 4.9.** An algebra *A* in a fusion category is called *separable* if the multiplication morphism *m* splits as an *A*-*A*-bimodule map. Intuitively, the internal hom [x, y] is in some sense the "maximal" one among all possible (Q, f).

There are two canonical morphisms defined by their mates shown as follows:

$$[y,z] \otimes [x,y] \to [x,z] \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad [y,z] \otimes [x,y] \otimes x \xrightarrow{\text{ev o}(1 \otimes \text{ev})} z \qquad (4.10)$$

$$\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{A}} \to [x,x] \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{A}} \otimes x \xrightarrow{\simeq} x. \qquad (4.11)$$

$$\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{A}} \to [x, x] \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{A}} \otimes x \xrightarrow{-} x. \tag{4.11}$$

When x = y = z, these morphisms endow [x, x] with the structure of an algebra.

**Example 4.18.** When  $\mathcal{A}$  is a fusion category and  $\mathcal{M} = \mathcal{A}$ , the algebra structure on [x, x] coincides with that of  $x \otimes x^*$  defined in Example 4.11.

<span id="page-24-2"></span>**Example 4.19.** Let A be a fusion category with the tensor product  $\otimes$  and A a simple separable algebra in A. The category  $A_A$  of right A-modules is naturally an indecomposable semisimple left A-module category with the left A-action  $\odot: A \times A_A \to A_A$  defined by  $(a, x) \mapsto a \otimes x$ . For  $x, y \in A_A$ ,  $[x, y]_A$  exists and we have

$$[x,y]_{\mathcal{A}} \simeq (x \otimes_{\mathcal{A}} y^*)^*. \tag{4.12}$$

In particular,  $[A, -] : A_A \to A$  is the precisely the forgetful functor.

The notion of full center can also be defined as an internal hom [Da]. Let A be a fusion category and  $\mathfrak{Z}(A)$  be its Drinfeld center. Let A be a simple separable algebra in A. Then the category  $A_{A|A}$  of A-A-bimodules in A is a fusion category [O]. There is an action functor  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{A}) \times \mathcal{A}_{A|A} \to \mathcal{A}_{A|A}$  defined by  $(z, x) \to z \otimes x$ .

<span id="page-24-0"></span>**Definition 4.20.** The *full center* Z(A) of A is the internal hom  $[A, A]_{\mathfrak{Z}(A)}$ .

#### <span id="page-25-0"></span>4.4 Unitary boundary-bulk CFT's

We have discussed the classification result of boundary-bulk rational CFT's. What about unitary rational CFT's? The representation theory of a unitary VOA has not been seriously studied until recently [Gui1, Gui2, Gui3]. As far as we know, the classification theory of unitary rational CFT's derived directly from Definition 4.1 or 4.4, is not yet available. There is, however, another formulation of a unitary rational CFT based on the representation theory of rational conformal nets, and its classification theory was known [LR, R1, R2]. It is essentially the same as that of boundary-bulk CFT's given in Theorem 4.7 but with a rational VOA replaced by a rational conformal net, whose module category is a UMTC, and the two types of Frobenius algebras replaced by two types of †-Frobenius algebras (see [KR3] for a review). A †-Frobenius algebra in a UMTC is a Frobenius algebra satisfying the conditions  $\Delta = m^{\dagger}$  and  $\epsilon = \eta^{\dagger}$ . For example, the Frobenius algebra constructed in (4.5) is a †-SSSFA. We will assume that this classification also works for the unitary boundary-bulk CFT's defined in Definition 4.1 or Definition 4.4. We make the classification result more precise below.

From now on, we assume that V is a unitary rational VOA of central charge c such that the category of unitary V-modules (see [Gui1]), still denoted by  $Mod_V$ , is a UMTC.

If  $\mathcal{B}$  is a UMTC and  $\mathcal{M}$  is an indecomposable unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module, then the category  $\operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M})$  of  $\mathcal{B}$ -module functors from  $\mathcal{M}$  to  $\mathcal{M}$  can be upgraded to a UFC [GHR]. Therefore, for non-zero  $x \in \mathcal{M}$ , the category  $\mathcal{B}_{[x,x]|[x,x]} \simeq \operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M})$  can be upgraded to a UFC. In this case, the simple separable algebra [x,x] is automatically a Frobenius algebra with  $\Delta := m^{\dagger}$  and  $\epsilon := \eta^{\dagger}$ . Moreover, it is a  $\dagger$ -SSSFA because  $m^{\dagger}$  is automatically a bimodule map by the unitarity of  $\mathcal{B}_{[x,x]|[x,x]}$ .

Conversely, all t-SSSFA's in  $\mathcal{B}$  can be realized by internal homs. Indeed, for a t-SSSFA A, the category  $\mathcal{B}_A$  of right A-modules is an indecomposable unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module. If x is a right A-module, then  $x^*$  is automatically a left A-module. For  $x, y \in \mathcal{B}_A$ , we have [O]

$$[x,y] = (x \otimes_A y^*)^* \simeq y \otimes_A x^*.$$

In particular,  $A \simeq [A, A]$  as Frobenius algebras.

If B is a  $\dagger$ -SSSFA in  $\mathcal B$  that is Morita equivalent to A, we can also realize B as an internal hom in the following way. Suppose that an invertible B-A-bimodule x defines the Morita equivalence. Since  $-\otimes_B x: \mathcal B_B \to \mathcal B_A$  defines an equivalence between the two left  $\mathcal B$ -modules and maps B to x, we obtain  $B \simeq [B, B] \simeq [x, x]$  as  $\dagger$ -SSSFA's.

For applications in unitary boundary-bulk CFT's with only V-invariant boundary conditions, we set  $\mathcal{B} = \operatorname{Mod}_V$ . For a given unitary boundary CFT A, i.e. a †-SSSFA in  $\mathcal{B}$ , all unitary boundary CFT's that share the same bulk with A are those †-SSSFA's in  $\mathcal{B}$  that are Morita equivalent to A [KR1]. Therefore, all of them can be recovered as internal homs [x,x] for  $x \in \mathcal{B}_A$ . The category  $\mathcal{B}_A$  is an indecomposable unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module, which is uniquely determined by the unitary bulk CFT Z(A) up to equivalence. Moreover, there is a bijection from the set of the equivalence classes of indecomposable unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -modules to that of the equivalence classes of Lagrangian algebras in  $\mathfrak{F}(B)$  defined by  $\mathcal{M} \mapsto [\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}}, \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}}]_{\mathfrak{F}(B)}$  [KR1, DMNO], where  $\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}}$  is the tensor unit of the UFC  $\mathrm{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M})$  and the action functor  $\mathfrak{F}(B) \times \mathrm{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M}) \to \mathrm{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M})$  is defined by  $(z,F) \to z \odot F(-)$ . When  $\mathcal{M} = \mathcal{B}_A$ , we have  $\mathrm{Fun}_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M},\mathcal{M}) = \mathcal{B}_{A|A}$  and  $\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}} = A$ .

Combining the above discussion with the assumption that unitary boundary-bulk CFT's based on VOA's is equivalent to those based on conformal nets [LR, R1, R2], we obtain the following physical "theorem".

<span id="page-25-1"></span>**Theorem**<sup>ph</sup> **4.21.** Let  $\mathcal{B} = \text{Mod}_V$  be a UMTC. For a given unitary bulk CFT  $A_{\text{bulk}}$  in  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{B})$  with a unique vacuum, we have the following assertions.

- The category of boundary conditions of  $A_{\text{bulk}}$  is given by an indecomposable unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module  $\mathcal{M}$  which is canonically associated to  $A_{\text{bulk}}$ . We have  $\mathcal{M} \simeq \mathcal{B}_A$  for a unitary boundary CFT A, i.e. a †-SSSFA in  $\mathcal{B}$  such that  $Z(A) \simeq A_{\text{bulk}}$  as algebras. An object  $x \in \mathcal{M}$  is called a boundary condition.
- $A_{\text{bulk}} = [id_{\mathcal{M}}, id_{\mathcal{M}}]_{\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{B})}$  is a Lagrangian algebra in  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{B})$ .
- For  $x \in \mathcal{M}$ , the unitary boundary CFT associated to the boundary condition x is given by  $[x, x]_{\mathcal{B}}$ , which is a t-SSFA in  $\mathcal{B}$  such that  $Z([x, x]_{\mathcal{B}}) \simeq A_{\text{bulk}}$ ;
- For  $x, y \in \mathcal{M}$ , the domain wall between the two boundary CFT's  $[x, x]_{\mathcal{B}}$  and  $[y, y]_{\mathcal{B}}$  is precisely given by the invertible bimodule  $[x, y]_{\mathcal{B}}$ .

**Remark 4.22.** What we are saying above is that upgrading a rational CFT (Theorem 4.10) to a unitary rational CFT (Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 4.21) amounts to replacing the adjective "Frobenius" by "†-Frobenius".

**Remark 4.23.** Strictly speaking, we should require not only the bulk CFT to have a unique vacuum, but each boundary CFT also to have a unique vacuum. Usually, a QFT (defined on  $S^n$  or  $\mathbb{R}^n$ ) with multiple vacuums is not stable. It will flow to a stable one (with a unique vacuum) under perturbation. This requirement amounts to consider only simple boundary conditions in  $\mathcal{M}$  and boundary CFT's  $[x,x]_{\mathcal{B}}$  for simple  $x \in \mathcal{M}$ , or equivalently, haploid †-SSSFA's A (i.e.  $\dim \hom_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}},A)=1$ ). For this reason, we sometimes discuss only simple boundary conditions and simple topological edge excitations in many physical discussions.

<span id="page-26-2"></span>**Remark 4.24.** For the category of boundary condition  $\mathfrak{M}$  of a given bulk CFT  $A_{\text{bulk}}$ , we have a  $\mathfrak{B}$ -enriched category  ${}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{M}$  via the canonical construction (see Example A.3). For a condensable algebra A in  $\mathfrak{B}$ , the category  $\mathfrak{M} := \overline{\mathfrak{B}}_A$  is a UFC and  $-\otimes A : \overline{\mathfrak{B}} \to \mathfrak{M}$  is a central functor. We obtain a  $\mathfrak{B}$ -enriched monoidal category  ${}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{M}$  via the canonical construction given in Theorem 5.3.

**Example 4.25.** The unique unitary rational VOA of central charge c=0 is  $V=\mathbb{C}$ . In this case, Theorem<sup>ph</sup> gives a classification of 1+1D unitary wall-boundary-bulk TQFT's. In particular, in this case,  $\mathcal{B}=\mathbf{H}$ , the only Lagrangian algebra in  $\mathbf{H}$  is  $\mathbb{C}$ . The category of boundary conditions associated to the algebra  $\mathbb{C}$  is  $\mathbf{H}$ , which is the unique indecomposable semisimple  $\mathbf{H}$ -module. For  $x,y\in\mathbf{H}$ , the associated boundary TQFT's are matrix algebras  $[x,x]=x\otimes_{\mathbb{C}}x^*=\mathrm{hom}_{\mathbf{H}}(x,x)$  and [y,y], respectively, and the domain wall between them is  $[x,y]=y\otimes x^*=\mathrm{hom}_{\mathbf{H}}(x,y)$ . Note that the full center of [x,x] is just the usual center  $Z([x,x])=\mathbb{C}$ .

<span id="page-26-1"></span>**Remark 4.26.** Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 4.21 plays a crucial in this work. We want physics readers to keep in mind that this radical simplification of wall-boundary-bulk CFT's to internal homs does not lose any physical information. More precisely, a single internal hom  $[x, y]_{\mathcal{B}}$  or  $[\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}}, \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{M}}]_{\mathfrak{J}(\mathcal{B})}$  (for  $\mathcal{B} = \mathrm{Mod}_V$ ) automatically includes the information of the spectrum, chiral (or non-chiral) fields, correlation functions, OPE, structure constants and partition functions on torus or any other higher genus surfaces, etc. The key point of this miracle is stated in Remark 4.8.

## <span id="page-27-1"></span><span id="page-27-0"></span>5.1 A natural construction of chiral gapless edge of $(\mathcal{B}, c)$

We are ready to give an explicit construction of a chiral gapless edge for a 2d chiral topological order defined by a pair  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$ , where  $\mathcal{B}$  is a UMTC realized by a unitary rational VOA V, i.e.  $\mathcal{B} = \text{Mod}_V$  and c is the central charge of V. We denote the tensor product in  $\mathcal{B}$  by  $\otimes = \otimes_V$ 

In this case, we give a very natural construction of a chiral gapless edge of the 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$ . We describe its ingredients explicitly as follows (also illustrated in Figure 9):

- Topological edge excitations are all obtained from moving topological bulk excitations to the edge. They are labeled by objects in B.
- $U = M_{1_{\mathcal{B}},1_{\mathcal{B}}} = [\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}},\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}] = \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}} = V$ , namely, all the boundary CFT's  $A_x$  and walls between them  $M_{x,y}$  are required to preserve the largest chiral symmetry V. As a consequence,  $M_{x,y}$  are objects in  $\mathcal{B}$ .
- $M_{x,y} = [x, y] = y \otimes x^* \in \mathcal{B}$ . In particular,  $M_{1,x} = [1, x] = x$ .
- Defect fields in  $M_{x,y}$  can be fused with those in  $M_{y,z}$  to give defect fields in  $M_{x,z}$ . This amounts to a morphism  $[y,z] \otimes [x,y] \rightarrow [x,z]$  in  $\mathcal{B}$ , which is defined as follows:

<span id="page-27-3"></span>
$$[y,z] \otimes [x,y] = z \otimes y^* \otimes y \otimes x^* \xrightarrow{1_z \otimes v_y \otimes 1_{x^*}} z \otimes x^* = [x,z]. \tag{5.1}$$

In this case, [x, y] is automatically an invertible [y, y]-[x, x]-bimodule.

• The spatial fusion of topological edge excitations  $M_{x',y'} \otimes M_{x,y} \to M_{x'\otimes x,y'\otimes y}$  (depicted in Figure 7) is a morphism in  $\mathcal{B}$  defined as follows:

<span id="page-27-2"></span>
$$[x', y'] \otimes [x, y] = y' \otimes x'^* \otimes y \otimes x^* \xrightarrow{1_{y'} \otimes c_{x'^*, y \otimes x^*}} (y' \otimes y) \otimes (x' \otimes x)^* = [x' \otimes x, y' \otimes y], \tag{5.2}$$

where we have used our braiding convention: the braiding  $c_{x',y\otimes x^*}: x'\otimes (y\otimes x^*)\to (y\otimes x^*)\otimes x'$  is defined by moving x' from  $t_{x'}>t_{yx^*}$  to  $t_{x'}< t_{yx^*}$  along a path lying entirely to the left of the world line supported on x in Figure 7.

**Remark 5.1.** This chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$  is the most studied edge in physics. But our description of the complete set of observables and their fusions in (3.5) and (5.2) is new.

<span id="page-27-4"></span>**Remark 5.2.** For a generic  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$ , it is not known if there exists any unitary rational VOA V of central charge c such that  $\mathcal{B} \simeq \operatorname{Mod}_V$ . If it indeed exists, it is not known how many are there? In a special case, when c = 0, the only unitary VOA of central charge zero is  $V = \mathbb{C}$  [Ga2]. It means that, in this case, a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{B}, 0)$  can have gapped edges  $(V = \mathbb{C})$  but does not have any non-trivial chiral gapless edges. It may have non-chiral gapless edges [CJKYZ, KZ4].

These observables on the above chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$  can be summarized by a pair  $(V, \mathcal{B}^{\sharp})$ , where  $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}$  is a  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched monoidal category defined as follows:

- an object in  $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}$  is a topological edge excitation, i.e. an object in  $\mathcal{B}$ ;
- $hom_{\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}}(x, y) = [x, y] = y \otimes x^*;$
- the identity morphism  $id_x : \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}} \to [x, x] = x \otimes x^*$  is defined by  $u_x : \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}} \to x \otimes x^*$ ;
- the composition morphism  $\otimes$  :  $[y,z] \otimes [x,y] \rightarrow [x,z]$  is defined by Eq. (5.1);
- the morphism  $\otimes$  :  $[x', y'] \otimes [x, y] \rightarrow [x' \otimes x, y' \otimes y]$  is defined by Eq. (5.2).

It was proved by Morrison and Penneys in [MP] that this categorical structure  $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}$  is indeed a  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched monoidal category. Moreover, it is just a special case of the so-called canonical construction.

<span id="page-28-0"></span>**Theorem 5.3** (Canonical Construction [MP]). Let  $\mathcal{D}$  be a braided monoidal category and  $\mathcal{Y}$  a monoidal category equipped with a braided oplax monoidal functor  $F_{\mathcal{Y}}: \overline{\mathcal{D}} \to \mathcal{Y}(\mathcal{Y})$  such that  $F_{\mathcal{Y}}(\mathbf{1}) = \mathbf{1}$ . It endows  $\mathcal{Y}$  with structure of an action functor  $\mathcal{O}: \mathcal{D} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{Y}$  defined by  $(c,y) \mapsto c \mathcal{O} y := F_{\mathcal{Y}}(c) \otimes y$ . We assume that internal hom [x,y] in  $\mathcal{D}$  exists for all  $x,y \in \mathcal{Y}$ . Then we obtain from the triple  $(\mathcal{D},\mathcal{Y},F_{\mathcal{Y}})$  a  $\mathcal{D}$ -enriched monoidal category, denoted by  $\mathcal{D}$  as follows:

- 1. objects in  $\mathcal{D}\mathcal{Y}$  are precisely the objects in  $\mathcal{Y}$ ;
- 2. for  $x, y \in \mathcal{Y}$ , hom  $\mathfrak{D}_{\mathcal{Y}}(x, y) := [x, y]$  in  $\mathfrak{D}$ ;
- 3. the identity morphism  $\mathrm{id}_x:\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{D}}\to[x,x]$  is the morphism in  $\mathcal{D}$  given by the mate of the canonical isomorphism  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{D}}\odot x\simeq x$  in  $\mathcal{Y}$ ;
- 4. the composition morphism  $\circ : [y, z] \otimes [x, y] \rightarrow [x, z]$  is the mate of the following composed morphism:

$$([y,z]\otimes[x,y])\odot x\to [y,z]\odot([x,y]\odot x)\xrightarrow{1_{[y,z]}\odot \mathrm{ev}}[y,z]\odot y\xrightarrow{\mathrm{ev}}z;$$

5.  $\otimes : [x', y'] \otimes [x, y] \rightarrow [x' \otimes x, y' \otimes y]$  is the mate of the following composed morphism:

$$\begin{split} ([x',y']\otimes[x,y])\odot(x'\otimes x) &= F_{\mathcal{V}}([x',y']\otimes[x,y])\otimes x'\otimes x\\ &\to F_{\mathcal{V}}([x',y'])\otimes F_{\mathcal{V}}([x,y])\otimes x'\otimes x\\ &\xrightarrow{1\otimes\beta_{F_{\mathcal{V}}([x,y]),x'}\otimes 1_x} F_{\mathcal{V}}([x',y'])\otimes x'\otimes F_{\mathcal{V}}([x,y])\otimes x\to y'\otimes y, \end{split}$$

where  $\beta_{F_{\mathcal{Y}}([x,y]),x'}: F_{\mathcal{Y}}([x,y]) \otimes x' \to x' \otimes F_{\mathcal{Y}}([x,y])$  is the half-braiding.

The underlying category of  ${}^{\mathcal{D}}\mathcal{Y}$  is  $\mathcal{Y}$ , and the background category of  ${}^{\mathcal{D}}\mathcal{Y}$  is  $\mathcal{D}$ .

Using the notation of the canonical construction, we see that  $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp} = {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B}$ . From now on, we denote  $(V, \mathcal{B}^{\sharp})$  by the pair  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B})$ . As we will show later, only physically relevant  $F_{\mathcal{Y}}$  are braided monoidal functors (NOT oplax). Hence, among all possible  $\mathcal{Y}$  (viewed as a finite left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module),  $\mathcal{Y} = \mathcal{B}$  is the canonical choice. For this reason, from now on, we will refer to  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B})$  as the canonical chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$ .

**Remark 5.4.** Strictly speaking, for a given 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$ , there is no canonical chiral gapless edge even if we assume the existence of a VOA V such that  $\mathcal{B} \simeq \operatorname{Mod}_V$  because such VOA's are often not unique (see [CMBCN] and [DM, EMS, LaL]). Once V is fixed, however, the categorical data  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B}$  is indeed canonical with respect to  $\mathcal{B}$ .

Imagine that we cut out the left half of a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{B},c)$  defined on  $\mathbb{R}^2$  by brutal force. One of the possible RG fixed point is the canonical chiral gapless edge  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B})$ . We will show later that, in general, there are other RG fixed points corresponding to different chiral gapless edges. What makes this picture so amazing is its holographical nature. That is, the information lost through the brutal force cutting in the spatial dimensions can be completely restored in the temporal dimension!

Therefore, we believe that this fictional folding process can not be just an ad hoc bookkeeping trick. It should have some yet-to-be-clarified deep physical meanings, and will be proved to be very useful later. Therefore, we would like to refer to this fictional folding trick as a "topological Wick rotation".

## <span id="page-32-0"></span>6 Chiral gapless edges II

In this section, we continue the analysis started in Section 3 on the structures and properties of observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge. This analysis leads us to a classification theory of all chiral gapless edges of any 2d topological orders.

## <span id="page-32-1"></span>6.1 $\chi^{\sharp}$ is an enriched unitary fusion category

At the end of Section 3, we conclude that the observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge of the 2d topological order  $(\mathfrak{C},c)$  can be summarized by a pair  $(V,\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp})$ , where V is the chiral symmetry and  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$  is an  $\mathrm{Mod}_V$ -enriched monoidal category. The objects in  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$  are topological edge excitations, and the hom spaces  $\mathrm{hom}_{\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}}(x,y) := M_{x,y}$  are boundary CFT's (if  $x \simeq y$ ) or domain walls between boundary CFT's (if  $x \not\simeq y$ ). In order to gain a better understanding of the hidden structure of  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$ , it is crucial to understand the mysterious relation between the objects in  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$  and the morphisms in  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$ . In terms of the underlying category  $\mathfrak{X}$  of  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$ , it amounts to ask for the relation between  $\mathfrak{X}$  and  $\mathfrak{B} = \mathrm{Mod}_V$ .

Consider the 1+1D world sheets depicted in Figure 10 (a), which is essentially the same as Figure 5 (b). The boundary CFT's  $A_{x_0}$ ,  $A_{y_0}$ ,  $A_{z_0}$  are compatible with U in the sense that they share the same bulk CFT Z(U). As we have shown at the end of Section 5, it is possible that there are  $x \in X$  such that  $M_{1,x} = 0$ , i.e. there is no boundary condition changing operator that can change the boundary condition associated to 1 to that associated to x. As a consequence, the whole enriched category  $X^{\sharp}$  splits into a direct sum of connected components:

$$\chi^{\sharp} \simeq \chi_0^{\sharp} \oplus \chi_1^{\sharp} \oplus \cdots \oplus \chi_N^{\sharp},$$

where  $\mathbf{1} \in \mathcal{X}_0^{\sharp}$  and  $M_{x_i,x_j} = 0$  for  $x_i \in \mathcal{X}_i^{\sharp}$ ,  $x_j \in \mathcal{X}_j^{\sharp}$ ,  $i,j=0,\cdots,N$  and  $i \neq j$ . Within each connected component  $\mathcal{X}_i^{\sharp}$ , all boundary CFT's and domain walls  $M_{x_i,y_i}$  (for  $x_i,y_i \in \mathcal{X}_i$ ) are necessarily compatible. Figure 10 (b) depicts three compatible boundary CFT's  $A_{x_i},A_{y_i},A_{z_i}$  for  $x_i,y_i,z_i \in \mathcal{X}_i^{\sharp}$  and their domain walls and the associated bulk CFT.

By the Naturality Principle in physics, if  $A_{x_i}$  appears as a boundary CFT on the 1+1D world sheet of the gapless edge  $(V, \mathfrak{X}^{\sharp})$ , then all boundary CFT's and their domain walls that are compatible with  $A_{x_i}$  should appear on this world sheet<sup>2</sup>. As a consequence, by Theorem 4.21 and Remark 4.24, each connected component  $\mathfrak{X}_i^{\sharp}$  of  $\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp}$ , is precisely the enriched category  ${}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X}_i$  via the canonical construction (see Example A.3), where  $\mathfrak{X}_i$  is an indecomposable unitary left  $\mathfrak{B}$ -module and, at the same time, the category of boundary conditions associated to the bulk CFT  $Z(A_{x_i})$ . Therefore, we obtain

$$\mathfrak{X}^{\sharp} \simeq {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X}_0 \oplus {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X}_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X}_N$$

as B-enriched categories and

$$\mathfrak{X} \simeq \mathfrak{X}_0 \oplus \mathfrak{X}_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathfrak{X}_N$$

as unitary left  $\mathcal{B}$ -modules. We denote the left  $\mathcal{B}$ -action on  $\mathcal{X}$  by  $\odot : \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X}$ . In other words,  $\mathcal{X}^{\sharp} \simeq {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}$  as  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched categories with  $[x,y]_{\mathcal{B}} \simeq M_{x,y}$  for  $x,y \in \mathcal{X}$ . We want to show that it is also an equivalence of enriched monoidal categories.

**Remark 6.1.** When  $V = \mathbb{C}$ ,  $\mathcal{B} = \mathbf{H}$  and the edge  $(\mathbb{C}, {}^{\mathbf{H}}\mathcal{X})$  is gapped. In this case,  $\mathbf{H}$  has a unique indecomposable unitary left  $\mathbf{H}$ -module  $\mathbf{H}$ . Therefore,  $\mathcal{X} \simeq \mathbf{H} \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathbf{H}$  as unitary left  $\mathbf{H}$ -modules, i.e.  $\mathcal{X}_i = \mathbf{H}$ . In this case,  $M_{x_i,x_j} = 0$  for  $x_i \in \mathcal{X}_i, x_j \in \mathcal{X}_j$  and  $i \neq j$ . For  $x_i \in \mathcal{X}_i = \mathbf{H}$ , the matrix algebra  $[x_i, x_i]_{\mathbf{H}} = \hom_{\mathcal{X}}(x_i, x_i)$  is actually a 0+1D boundary TQFT, which has a trivial bulk TQFT given by its center  $Z([x_i, x_i]) = \mathbb{C}$ . The ordinary center is also the full center in this case.

**Remark 6.2.** By Theorem 4.21 and [DKR, Proposition 7.3], the result  $X^{\sharp} \simeq {}^{\mathfrak{B}}X$  implies that

$$M_{y_i,z_i} \otimes_{M_{y_i,y_i}} M_{x_i,y_i} \simeq M_{x_i,z_i}$$
, for non-zero objects  $x_i, y_i, z_i \in X_i$ .

Morrison-Penneys' classification of  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched monoidal categories [MP] indicates that  $\mathcal{X}^{\sharp}$  is necessarily obtained from a triple  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{X}, F_{\mathcal{X}})$  via the canonical construction, where  $F_{\mathcal{X}} : \overline{\mathcal{B}} \to \mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{X})$  is a braided oplax monoidal functor such that the composed functor  $L : \overline{\mathcal{B}} \xrightarrow{F_{\mathcal{X}}} \mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{X}) \xrightarrow{f} \mathcal{X}$  is left adjoint to a functor  $M_{1,-} : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{B}$ , which will be defined later, i.e.

$$\overline{\mathcal{B}} \xrightarrow{F_{\mathcal{X}}} 3(\mathcal{X}) \tag{6.1}$$

In our case, since  $\mathfrak{X}$  is already a unitary left  $\mathfrak{B}$ -module with  $[x,y]_{\mathfrak{B}}=M_{x,y}$ , the result turns out to be even stronger:  $F_{\mathfrak{X}}$  is a unitary braided monoidal functor (NOT oplax), or equivalently, L is a central functor (recall Diagram (2.5)). To see this, let us go through some parts of Morrison-Penneys' proof that are adapted and simplified to suit our case.

1. There is a well-defined  $\mathbb{C}$ -linear functor  $M_{1,-}: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{B}$  defined by  $y \mapsto M_{1,y}$  and

$$f \mapsto (M_{1,y} \xrightarrow{\simeq} V \otimes_V M_{1,y} \xrightarrow{f \otimes_V 1} M_{y,y'} \otimes_V M_{1,y} \xrightarrow{\circledcirc} M_{1,y'}) \quad \text{for } f : y \to y' \text{ in } \mathfrak{X}.$$

It is clear that  $M_{1-}(X_i) = 0$  for i > 0.

- 2. Let  $L: \mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{X}$  be the left adjoint functor of  $M_{1,-}$ . The category of topological edge excitations  $\mathcal{X}$  is monoidal. The natural physical requirement is that each topological edge excitation should have its anti-particle. This requires  $\mathcal{X}$  to be rigid thus a UFC. We want to show that L is a central functor.
- 3. By definition (see [MP, Sec. 2.7]),  $\mathcal{X}^{\sharp}$  is rigid if and only if  $\mathcal{X}$  is rigid. The rigidity of  $\mathcal{X}^{\sharp}$  implies the Frobenius reciprocity:  $M_{x,y\otimes z^{*}} \simeq M_{x\otimes z,y} \simeq M_{z,x^{*}\otimes y}$  [MP, Sec. 2.7]. In particular, we have

<span id="page-34-0"></span>
$$M_{x,y} \simeq M_{1,x^* \otimes y} \simeq M_{1,y \otimes x^*} \simeq M_{y^*,x^*}.$$
 (6.2)

4. We have  $L(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}) \simeq \mathbf{1}$  because  $\hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(L(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}), x) \simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{B}}(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}, M_{\mathbf{1}, x}) \simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(\mathbf{1}, x)$  for  $x \in \mathfrak{X}$ . Moreover, we have

$$\hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(L(a)\otimes x,y)\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(L(a),y\otimes x^*)\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{B}}(a,M_{1,y\otimes x^*})\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{B}}(a,M_{x,y})\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(a\odot x,y).$$

Thus  $L(a) \otimes x \simeq a \odot x$  for  $a \in \mathcal{B}$  and  $x, y \in \mathcal{X}$ . Then  $L(a) \simeq a \odot 1$ . This further implies the following isomorphisms:

$$L(a) \otimes L(b) \simeq a \odot (b \odot \mathbf{1}) \simeq L(a \otimes b).$$

Therefore, *L* is monoidal.

5. The monoidal functor  $L : \overline{\mathbb{B}} \to \mathfrak{X}$  is a central functor. This amounts to show that each L(a) can be endowed with the structure of a half-braiding [DMNO]. Indeed, we have

$$\hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(L(a)\otimes x,y)\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{B}}(a,M_{x,y})\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{B}}(a,M_{1,x^*\otimes y})\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(L(a),x^*\otimes y)\simeq \hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(x\otimes L(a),y).$$

Therefore, we obtain a half-braiding isomorphism  $L(a) \otimes x \simeq x \otimes L(a)$  as desired by Yoneda Lemma. Further details of the proof could be found in [MP].

6. Moreover, by the physical requirement, the natural isomorphisms in Eq. (6.2) should be unitary<sup>3</sup>. $$M_{x',\mathbf{1}} \otimes_U M_{x,\mathbf{1}} \simeq M_{x' \otimes x,\mathbf{1}}, \quad \text{for } x,x' \in \mathfrak{X}_0.$$

also holds. As a consequence, the spatial fusion of  $M_{x',y'}$  and  $M_{x,y}$  can be achieved by fusing  $M_{1,y'}$ ,  $M_{x',1}$ ,  $M_{1,y}$ ,  $M_{x,1}$  as shown in Figure 11. The braided interchange property (recall Diagram 3.8) implies that the morphism (3.6) must be an isomorphism:

<span id="page-36-3"></span>
$$M_{x',y'} \otimes_U M_{x,y} \simeq M_{x'\otimes x,y'\otimes y}, \qquad \text{for } x, x', y, y' \in \mathcal{X}_0.$$
 (6.4)

This formula does not hold for general  $x, x', y, y' \in \mathcal{X}$ . This also explain why (5.2) is an isomorphism because  $\mathcal{X} = \mathcal{X}_0$  for the canonical chiral gapless edge. When V = 0 (i.e. edge is gapped),  $\mathcal{X}_0 = \mathbf{H}$  and (6.4) simply says that the tensor product of two matrix algebras is again a matrix algebra. More formula of this type will be given and studied in [KYZ].

The results in this subsection motivates us to introduce the following definition.

<span id="page-36-0"></span>**Definition 6.4.** An enriched monoidal category  ${}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathcal{X}$  obtained by the canonical construction from a triple  $(\mathfrak{B}, \mathcal{X}, F_{\mathcal{X}})$  is called a  $\mathfrak{B}$ -enriched (unitary) multi-fusion category if

- 1. B is a (unitary) braided multi-fusion category;
- 2. X is a (unitary) multi-fusion category;
- 3.  $F_{\mathfrak{X}}: \overline{\mathcal{B}} \to \mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{X})$  is a (unitary) braided monoidal functor.

It is called a  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched (unitary) fusion category if both  $\mathcal{B}$  and  $\mathcal{X}$  are also fusion categories.

Then the results in this subsection can be summarized as follows:

• A chiral gapless edge  $(V, \mathfrak{X}^{\sharp})$  of a 2d topological order  $(\mathfrak{C}, c)$  is precisely given by  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X})$ , where  $\mathfrak{B} = \operatorname{Mod}_V$  is a UMTC and  ${}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X}$  is a  $\mathfrak{B}$ -enriched unitary fusion category.

### <span id="page-37-0"></span>6.2 Classification theory of chiral gapless edges

The last constraint on the mathematical description of a chiral gapless edge is that it must satisfy the so-called boundary-bulk relation, which says that the center of a gapped/gapless edge should coincide with the UMTC describing the bulk phase [KWZ2]. Motivated by the physical meaning of the boundary-bulk relation for 2d topological orders, we introduced the notion of the center of an enriched monoidal category in [KZ2]. We will not give the precise definition of this notion in this work. Instead, we will do that in Part II [KZ4].

<span id="page-37-2"></span>**Theorem 6.5** ([KZ2]). For a  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched fusion category  $\mathcal{B}X$  obtained from a triple  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{X}, F_{\mathcal{X}})$ , its center  $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{B}X)$  is given by the centralizer of the image of  $\overline{\mathcal{B}}$  in  $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X})$ , denoted by  $F_{\mathcal{X}}(\overline{\mathcal{B}})'|_{\mathcal{B}(X)}$ .

Therefore, the requirement of boundary-bulk relation, i.e.  $\mathfrak{J}(^{\mathbb{B}}\mathfrak{X}) \simeq \mathbb{C}$ , is equivalent to the condition:  $F_{\mathfrak{X}}(\overline{\mathbb{B}})'|_{\mathfrak{J}(\mathfrak{X})} \simeq \mathbb{C}$  as UMTC's. Since  $\overline{\mathbb{B}}$  is a UMTC, the functor  $F_{\mathfrak{X}}$  is necessarily a braided monoidal embedding. Therefore, we obtain  $\overline{\mathbb{B}} \boxtimes \mathbb{C} \simeq \mathfrak{J}(\mathfrak{X})$  as UMTC's [DMNO]. Similar to gapped edges, to uniquely determine the edge, we need specify how the topological excitations in the bulk are mapped to those on the edge [Ko6]. This is given by a central functor  $\mathbb{C} \to \mathfrak{X}$  (recall Diagram (2.5)). Together with  $F_{\mathfrak{X}}$ , we obtain a unitary braided monoidal equivalence:

<span id="page-37-1"></span>
$$\phi_{\mathcal{X}}: \overline{\mathcal{B}} \boxtimes \mathcal{C} \xrightarrow{\simeq} \mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{X}), \tag{6.5}$$

which should be viewed as a defining data of the edge. This completes our analysis.

**Remark 6.6.** The existence of a braided monoidal equivalence  $\phi_{\mathcal{X}}$  in (6.5) should be viewed as an anomaly-free condition. Namely, the bulk ( $\mathcal{C}$ , c) should resolve all the anomalies of the edge (V,  $\mathcal{X}$ ) viewed as an anomalous 1d phase.

How do we know whether we have found all the physical requirements of  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathfrak{X}$ ? We have indeed found all of them because the pairs  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathfrak{X})$  are precisely those chiral gapless edges constructed in Section 5.2 via the physical process of fusing canonical gapless edges with gapped domain walls (with a loophole to be fixed in Section 6.3). In other words, topological Wick rotations realize all chiral gapless edges.

We have found the precise mathematical description of the observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge as a pair  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X})$ . We need to answer a few physical questions before we claims that this mathematical description actually classify all chiral gapless edges.

- 1. What do we mean by a chiral gapless edge physically? In reality, if a chiral gapless edge of a 2d topological order is realized in lab, it might not have any topological edge excitations on it at all before we introduce topological defects (or impurities) onto the edge. In this context, by a chiral gapless edge described by  $(V, {}^{\mathbb{B}}\mathfrak{X})$ , we mean the maximal way of inserting topological defects onto the edge without breaking the chiral symmetry V.
- 2. How do we know different pairs describes different edges? We provide three answers. First, two different edges can be obtained by fusing canonical edges with two different gapped domain walls along two potentially different 2d topological orders. Therefore, a transition between two chiral gapless edges can be understood as a gap-closing topological phase transition between two 2d topological orders, together with a gap-closing transition between two gapped domain walls. Secondly, for two different  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}$  and  ${}^{\mathcal{B}'}\mathcal{X}'$ , the categories of topological edge excitations are different; mathematically,  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}$  and  ${}^{\mathcal{B}'}\mathcal{X}'$ , as two  $E_1$ -algebras, can be viewed as different topological invariants defined on open 1-disks. They lead to different global topological invariant by integration or factorization homology [AKZ]. Changing of topological invariants is associated with a phase transition in general. Secondly, for a given chiral gapless edge  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X})$ , when we introduce more defects that break the chiral symmetry V to a smaller one (see Remark 6.11), we believe that this process of breaking of chiral symmetry should cause a purely edge phase transition.

Figure 12: These two pictures depict a physical description of pure edge phase transition via a topological Wick rotation.

- <span id="page-38-1"></span>3. How to define a phase transition between two 1+1D gapless phase? A phase transition between two gapped phases are defined by closing the gap. But a phase transition between two gapless phases is not so clear from this perspective. As far as we know, there is no model-independent definition of such a phase transition. Interestingly, the physical intuition of chiral gapless edges actually provides us three possible model-independent definitions of a phase transition between two potentially anomalous 1+1D gapless phases.
  - (a) We can define such a phase transition as a process of changing or breaking local quantum symmetries (i.e. chiral symmetries in this case).
  - (b) Since all chiral gapless edges can be obtained by fusing the canonical chiral gapless edges with gapped domain walls along two 2d bulk phases, we can define the phase transition between two chiral gapless edges by a gap-closing topological phase transition between two bulk phases and that between two gapped domain walls.
  - (c) We can define such a 1+1D phase transition by a 2d topological phase transition via a topological Wick rotation. More precisely, a 2d topological phase transition from a 2d topological order (B, *c*) to a new one (D, *c*) can be achieved by first introducing some islands of (D, *c*)-phases into the 2d topological order (B, *c*) as depicted in the first picture in Figure [12.](#page-38-1) When these islands proliferated, a topological phase transition from (B, *c*) to (D, *c*) occurs. By Wick rotating this process, we obtain a description (or a definition) of a purely edge 1+1D phase transition as depicted in the second picture in Figure [12.](#page-38-1) This definition automatically makes sense in any dimensions. We will return to this point in Section [7.](#page-42-0)

As a consequence of these definitions, any chiral gapless edge (*V* 0 , B0 X 0 ) can be obtained from another one (*V*, <sup>B</sup>X) via a purely edge phase transition.

We conclude that the mathematical description of observables on a 1+1D world sheet of a chiral gapless edge as a pair (*V*, <sup>B</sup>X) provides a classification of all chiral gapless edges. We summarize this result as a physical theorem.

<span id="page-38-0"></span>**Theorem**ph **6.7.** Gapped and chiral gapless edges of a 2d topological order (C, *c*) are precisely described and classified by pairs (*V*, <sup>B</sup>X), where

- *V* is a unitary rational VOA of central charge *c* such that B := Mod*<sup>V</sup>* is a UMTC;
- X is a UFC equipped with a unitary braided monoidal equivalence φ<sup>X</sup> : B -C → Z(X);
- <sup>B</sup>X is the B-enriched UFC obtained via the canonical construction from the triple (B, X, *F*X), where *F*<sup>X</sup> is the unitary braided monoidal functor defined as follows:

$$F_{\mathcal{X}}: \overline{\mathcal{B}} \hookrightarrow \overline{\mathcal{B}} \boxtimes \mathcal{C} \xrightarrow{\phi_{\mathcal{X}}} \mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{X}).$$

Equivalently, for the convenience of numerical computation,

• all gapped and chiral gapless edges of  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  are classified by pairs (V, A), where A is a Lagrangian algebra in  $\overline{\text{Mod}}_V \boxtimes \mathcal{C}$ .

When  $V = \mathbb{C}$ , the edge is gapped. In this case,  $Mod_V = H$ .

**Remark 6.8.** Unstable edges naturally occur if we fuse a chiral gapless edge with a gapped domain wall. If we allow unstable edges in our mathematical description, we can simply replace the condition that  $\mathcal{X}$  is UFC by a weaker condition that  $\mathcal{X}$  is a unitary multi-fusion category (see also Remark 6.15).

**Remark 6.9.** There is a nice classification of gapless edges for abelian 2d topological orders given in [CCMNPY]. It will be very interesting to compare their results with ours. Constructing new gapless edges via anyon condensations of a non-abelian bulk phase  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  was considered in some special cases in [BN]. Our constructions of gapless edges are more general than those obtained by condensing the bulk [DMNO, Ko6].

**Example 6.10.** Consider a conformal embedding  $V \subset A$  of unitary rational VOA's of central charge c, e.g.

```

By topological Wick rotations, we obtain

- a chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{B}, c)$  defined by  $(A, {}^{\text{Mod}_A}(\mathcal{B}_A))$ , in which  $M_{1,1} = A$ ;
- a chiral gapless edge of  $(\text{Mod}_A, c)$  defined by  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{B}_A^{\text{rev}}))$ , in which  $M_{1,1} = A$ .

<span id="page-39-0"></span>**Remark 6.11.** Recall Remark 3.7, for a given chiral gapless edge, the chiral symmetry is chosen to be the maximal one that is transparent on the entire 1+1D world sheet of the edge. If we choose a smaller one, i.e.  $V' \subseteq V$ , then the category of boundary conditions for the same bulk CFT Z(U) will be enlarged to  $(\operatorname{Mod}_{V'})_U$ . Note that  $(\operatorname{Mod}_V)_U \simeq ((\operatorname{Mod}_{V'})_V^0)_U \subseteq (\operatorname{Mod}_{V'})_U$ . It is not yet possible to list explicitly all chiral gapless edges for a given chiral 2d topological order because, for a fixed UMTC  $\mathcal{B}$ , how many unitary rational VOA's V satisfy  $\mathrm{Mod}_V \simeq \mathcal{B}$  as UMTC's is still an open question. It was conjectured that every UMTC  $\mathcal{C}$  (without fixing c) can be realized by the category of unitary modules over a unitary VOA (see for example [Ga1, TeW]). It is not true if we also fix c. More precisely, for a pair  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ , in general, it is not possible to find a VOA V of central charge c such that  $\mathcal{C} \simeq \mathrm{Mod}_V$ . For example, when  $\mathcal{C}$  is non-chiral and  $\mathcal{C} \not\simeq \mathbf{H}$ , such V does not exist because the only unitary VOA of central charge 0 is the trivial one  $V = \mathcal{C}$ . In other words, the non-chiral 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{C}, 0)$  does not have any chiral gapless edges. It has only gapped and non-chiral gappable gapless edges (recall Remark 5.2). It is, however, possible to find V of central charges of  $8\mathbb{Z}$  such that  $\mathcal{C} \simeq \mathrm{Mod}_V$  [Ga1]. It is interesting to note that our classification result actually supports a different conjecture:

**Conjecture 6.12.** For a UMTC  $\mathcal{C}$  and a central charge c such that  $c_{\mathcal{C}}^{\text{top}} = c \pmod{8}$ , there is at least one unitary VOA V of central charge c such that  $\text{Mod}_V$  is a UMTC Witt equivalent to  $\mathcal{C}$ .

**Remark 6.13.** Interestingly, if there are only finitely many chiral gapless edges of a given bulk phase  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ , an assumption which is not totally unreasonable, then our theory suggests that there are only finitely many unitary rational VOA's with central charge c such that their module categories are UMTC's that are Witt equivalent to  $\mathcal{C}$ .

### <span id="page-40-2"></span><span id="page-40-0"></span>6.3 Universality at RG fixed points

The reasoning behind our classification theory of chiral gapless edges given in Section 6.2 will not be complete unless we fill the one last loophole. Note that we have not yet provided any explanation of the mysterious formula (5.6). We will do that in this subsection.

Instead of explaining the mysterious formula (5.6), we would like to explain a more general formula, which describes the fusion of two chiral gapless domain walls (recall Remark 6.14). We illustrate two chiral gapless walls before the fusion in Figure 13 (a) and after the fusion in Figure 13 (b). More precisely,  $\mathcal{A}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$ ,  $\mathcal{C}$ ,  $\mathcal{D}$ ,  $\mathcal{E}$  are UMTC's, and  $\mathcal{M}$  and  $\mathcal{N}$  are UFC's equipped with unitary braided monoidal equivalences  $\phi_{\mathcal{M}}:\overline{\mathcal{C}}\boxtimes\overline{\mathcal{A}}\boxtimes\mathcal{D}\xrightarrow{\cong}\mathfrak{J}(\mathcal{M})$  and  $\phi_{\mathcal{N}}:\overline{\mathcal{D}}\boxtimes\overline{\mathcal{B}}\boxtimes\mathcal{E}\xrightarrow{\cong}\mathfrak{J}(\mathcal{N}).^5$  The vertical direction is the direction of time. Two vertical planes depict the 1+1D world sheets (or fictional bulk phases) of two chiral gapless domain walls  $(V_{\mathcal{A}},{}^{\mathcal{A}}\mathcal{M})$  and  $(V_{\mathcal{B}},{}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{N})$ . Note that  $V_{\mathcal{A}}$  and  $V_{\mathcal{B}}$  have central charges  $c_2$  and  $c_3$ , respectively. They precisely make up the difference of the chiral central charges of the two sides of the wall. Now we propose a formula for the spatial fusion of these two walls:

<span id="page-40-4"></span>
$$(V_{\mathcal{A}}, {}^{\mathcal{A}}\mathcal{M}) \boxtimes_{(\mathcal{D}, c_1 + c_2)} (V_{\mathcal{B}}, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{N}) = (V_{\mathcal{A}} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} V_{\mathcal{B}}, {}^{\mathcal{A} \boxtimes \mathcal{B}}(\mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{N})). \tag{6.6}$$

Note that this formula covers the formula (5.6) as a special case.

<span id="page-40-1"></span>**Remark 6.15.** Note that  $\mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{N}$  is in general a multi-fusion category even if both  $\mathcal{M}$  are  $\mathcal{N}$  are fusion ones. In this case, it describes an unstable domain wall, which can flow to a stable one under RG flow [AKZ]. Since the multi-fusion categories naturally appear in such a fusion process, it is also natural to use unitary multi-fusion categories to describe the category of topological wall excitations.

Before we explain the formula (6.6), we first recall that  $M_{x,y} = [x, y]_{\mathbb{B}}$ . By Definition 4.16,  $M_{x,y}$  is universal in the mathematical sense. More precisely,  $[x, y]_{\mathbb{B}}$ , as a space of boundary condition changing operators, is equipped with a map ev :  $[x, y]_{\mathbb{B}} \odot x \rightarrow y$ , which specifies how the boundary condition changing is done. For any space of boundary condition changing

operators, i.e.  $(Q, f: Q \odot x \to y)$ , where Q is the space and f specifies how the boundary condition changing is done, there exists a unique morphism  $\underline{f}: Q \to [x,y]_{\mathbb{B}}$  exhibiting the following diagram

<span id="page-41-1"></span>
$$[x, y]_{\mathcal{B}} \odot x$$

**Principle of Universality at RG fixed points**: A physical theory at a RG fixed point always satisfies a proper universal property in the mathematical sense.

Another example of the Principle of Universality is the boundary-bulk relation, i.e. bulk = the center of a boundary [KWZ2]. More precisely, the unique gapped n+1d bulk  $B(\mathfrak{X})$  of a (gapped or gapless) nd boundary  $\mathfrak{X}$  satisfies the following universal property:

<span id="page-41-0"></span>
$$B(\mathcal{X}) \boxtimes \mathcal{X}$$

$$\exists ! \underline{g} \boxtimes \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{X}}$$

$$Q \boxtimes \mathcal{X} \xrightarrow{g} \mathcal{X}.$$

$$(6.8)$$

where  $B(\mathfrak{X})$  is viewed as an nd topological order by forgetting additional structures,  $\boxtimes$  is the stacking operation of two phases, and both m and g are morphisms between potentially anomalous nd (gapped or gapless) phases (introduced in [KWZ2]) and define a unital  $B(\mathfrak{X})$ -action and a unital  $\mathfrak{Q}$ -action on  $\mathfrak{X}$ , respectively. When  $\mathfrak{X}$  is a gapped boundary of a 2d topological order, the 2d bulk  $B(\mathfrak{X})$  is precisely given by the Drinfeld center  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{X})$  of  $\mathfrak{X}$ , which is viewed as a unitary fusion category. Mathematically, in this case,  $\boxtimes$  is the Deligne tensor product; m is just the tensor product functor  $\otimes$  of  $\mathfrak{X}$ , and is unital and monoidal; and the unital action g is also monoidal; and (6.8) is nothing but the mathematical universal property of the Drinfeld center.

More examples will appear below. Note that the condition "at RG fixed point" is crucial. This principle can not be true for general QFT's or many-body condensed matter systems that are not at RG fixed point. However, for general QFT's, we believe that observables (such as those in the bulks, boundaries, walls or other defects, instantons, etc.) should factor through the universal ones.

Now we return to the physical situation depicted in Figure 13. A typical example of physical observables on the 1+1D world sheet of each gapless wall before the fusion are  $[x,y]_A$  for  $x,y \in \mathcal{M}$  and  $[p,q]_B$  for  $p,q \in \mathcal{N}$ . If we treat two vertical planes as two real bulk phases in space, then, when we fuse them, the observables  $[x,y]_A$  and  $[p,q]_B$  should also fuse and give  $[x,y]_A \boxtimes [p,q]_B \in \mathcal{A} \boxtimes \mathcal{B}$  (see Figure 13 (b)). This naive picture contradicts to both the formula (6.6) and the Principle of Universality.

It is again a typical quantum quenching scenario. When we fuse two walls in the spatial dimensions, only thing we should expect is that the categories of wall excitations  $\mathbb M$  and  $\mathbb N$  are fused together to give  $\mathbb M \boxtimes_{\mathbb D} \mathbb N$ . Indeed, the topological excitations in  $\mathbb D$  acts on those in  $\mathbb M$  from right according to  $(m,d)\mapsto m\odot d$  and on those in  $\mathbb N$  from left according to  $(d,n)\mapsto d\odot n$ . Assume that this fusion produces a functor F from  $\mathbb M \boxtimes \mathbb N$  to the not-yet-known category of topological excitations in the final wall. We should expect that  $F(m\odot d,n)\simeq F(m,d\odot n)$  for  $m\in \mathbb M$ ,  $n\in \mathbb N$ . Such F is called a  $\mathbb D$ -balanced functor. The relative tensor product  $\boxtimes_{\mathbb D}: \mathbb M \times \mathbb N \to \mathbb M \boxtimes_{\mathbb D} \mathbb N$ , defined by  $(m,n)\mapsto m\boxtimes_{\mathbb D} n$ , is an example of such  $\mathbb D$ -balanced functors, i.e.  $(m\odot d)\boxtimes_{\mathbb D} n\simeq m\boxtimes_{\mathbb D} (d\odot n)$ , and is universal among all  $\mathbb D$ -balanced functors. That is, for any  $\mathbb D$ -balanced functor  $G: \mathbb M \boxtimes \mathbb N \to \mathbb Y$ ,

there exists a unique  $G: \mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{N} \to \mathcal{Y}$  such that the following diagram

$$\mathcal{M} \boxtimes \mathcal{N} \xrightarrow{\boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}}} \mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{N}$$

$$\downarrow^{\exists!} \underline{G}$$

is commutative. By the Principle of Universality, we conclude that the category of topological excitations on the wall after the fusion is given by  $\mathbb{M} \boxtimes_{\mathbb{D}} \mathbb{N}$ . It is worthwhile to remind the readers that this is precisely how two gapped walls are fused [KK, FSV, Ko6]. As a consequence, by [KZ1, Theorem 3.3.6] and the boundary-bulk relation (recall Theorem 6.5), the background category of the wall after the fusion has to be  $\mathbb{A} \boxtimes \mathbb{B}$ .

Assume that this fusion happens at t=0. Then two wall excitations  $x\in \mathbb{M}$  and  $p\in \mathbb{N}$  are fused into a new wall excitation  $xp:=x\boxtimes_{\mathbb{D}}p\in \mathbb{M}\boxtimes_{\mathbb{D}}\mathbb{N}$ . According to the discussion in Section 3.2, observables living on the world line support on it will be changed to the boundary CFT  $M_{xp,xp}$ , which is precisely given by  $[xp,xp]_{A\boxtimes \mathbb{B}}$ . Similarly, topological excitations  $y\in \mathbb{M}$  and  $q\in \mathbb{N}$  is fused to  $yq:=y\boxtimes_{\mathbb{D}}q$ . Therefore, the domain wall between the two boundary conditions xp and yq should be given by  $M_{xp,yq}=[xp,yq]_{A\boxtimes \mathbb{B}}$ .

In general, the spatial fusion of instantons  $[x, y]_A \boxtimes [p, q]_B$  is not invariant under RG flow<sup>6</sup>, which will drive it to a new RG fixed point given by  $[xp, yq]_{A\boxtimes B}$ . Indeed,  $[x, y]_A \boxtimes [p, q]_B$  is a space of boundary condition changing operators because it is equipped with a natural way to do the "boundary condition changing":

$$([x,y]_{\mathcal{A}}\boxtimes [p,q]_{\mathcal{B}})\odot (x\boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}}p):=([x,y]_{\mathcal{A}}\odot x)\boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}}([p,q]_{\mathcal{B}}\odot p)\xrightarrow{\operatorname{ev}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}}\operatorname{ev}}y\boxtimes_{\mathcal{D}}q.$$

Therefore, there exists a canonical morphism  $g:[x,y]_A\boxtimes [p,q]_B\to [xp,yq]$  by the universal property of the internal hom (6.7). When g is not an isomorphism, it means that additional operators such as excitations tunneling between two 1d walls becoming local as they getting close, and it is a manifestation of the spatial fusion anomaly and, at the same time, a mathematical description of the RG flow. The appearance of spatial fusion anomaly is due to the fact that both domain walls are anomalous 1d phases. This fact allows additional operators to become local as two 1d walls getting close. Indeed, when both of the 1d phases are anomaly-free, what live on two world sheets are modular invariant bulk CFT's. In this case, g is indeed an isomorphism, or equivalently, spatial fusion anomaly vanishes.

In summary, we have provided an explanation of the formula (6.6) in terms of RG flow and Principle of Universality. Therefore, the reasoning behind our main result Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 6.7 is complete.

### <span id="page-42-0"></span>7 Conclusions and Outlooks

In this work, for a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ , we have found a complete and precise mathematical description of all observables on the 1+1D world sheet of a 1d chiral gapless edge. This description also provides a mathematical classification of all chiral gapless edges as summaried in Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 6.7. As a consequence, all chiral gapless edges can be obtained from topological Wick rotations.

The significance of this work is manifold. As we will show in [KZ4], the mathematical theory of chiral gapless edges immediately implies a theory of non-chiral gapless edges and a generalization of the old boundary-bulk relation [KZ1, Theorem 3.3.7.] to include gapless edges.

On the one hand, we have  $[\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}, \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{B}}]_{\mathcal{B}} \boxtimes [\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{M}}, \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{M}}]_{\mathcal{B}} = U \neq V$  in general (see Section 5.2).

Besides these immediate generalizations, there are more surprising and exciting implications of this work.

- 1. It provides a new and systematic way of describing and classifying all gapless edges of 2d symmetry protected/enriched topological orders.
- 2. It provides a new and systematic way of studying all 1+1D phase transitions among gapped or gapless 1d phases.

More importantly, this work also suggests that we can study higher dimensional gapless boundaries similarly because many of the physical arguments used in this works, such as dimensional reductions, topological Wick rotations and Principle of Universality at RG fixed points, should work automatically in any dimensions.

- 1. As we mentioned in Section [2.3,](#page-7-0) the main results of [\[KWZ2\]](#page-49-7) says that a topological order in any dimension is determined by one of its boundaries by taking center regardless if this boundary is gapped or gapless (see [\[KWZ2,](#page-49-7) Remark 5.7]). As a consequence, if an *n*+1d topological order has a precise mathematical description based on higher categories (see [\[KW,](#page-49-8) [KWZ1\]](#page-49-18)), so does its *n*d gapless boundary. This prediction is already highly non-trivial and rather surprising. In particular, this work can be viewed as a consequence of this prediction.
- 2. This work also suggests that we should be able to construct gapless boundaries of higher dimensional topological orders via the topological Wick rotations together with additional information of local quantum symmetries such as the chiral symmetry in this work. Note that the topological Wick rotation depicted in [\(5.7\)](#page-30-3) automatically makes sense in all dimensions. This provides a new way to construct and describe potentially anomalous gapless phases in higher dimensions by certain enriched higher categories. Although the topological Wick rotation alone can not determine local quantum symmetries, it does provide a severe constraint on the possible local quantum symmetries. Moreover, many results can be derived without knowing local quantum symmetries. If this surprising speculation is indeed correct, it will be a very exciting progress for a systematic study of gapless phases in higher dimensions. Moreover, this work also suggests that a phase transition between two *n*+1D gapless phases can be defined by a gap-closing *n*+2D topological phase transitions via topological Wick rotations as illustrated in Figure [12,](#page-38-1) which automatically makes sense in any dimensions.

We summarize above observations by a correspondence between gapped and gapless phases. It will be called Gapped-Gapless Correspondence, and will serve as a guiding principle for our future studies.

**Gapped-Gapless Correspondence**: All *n*d gapless boundaries (or potentially anomalous *n*d gapless phases), including higher codimensional domain walls, of an *n*+1d topological order can be obtained from topological Wick rotations together with additional information of local quantum symmetries. Moreover, a phase transition between two gapless boundaries (without altering the bulk topological order) can be defined by a gap-closing topological phase transition via a topological Wick rotation (see Figure [12\)](#page-38-1).

The typical structures of local quantum symmetries for QFT's in higher dimension will be briefly discussed in [\[KZ4,](#page-50-0) Remark 3.1].
