# A FIELD GUIDE TO CATEGORIES WITH $A_n$ FUSION RULES

#### CAIN EDIE-MICHELL AND SCOTT MORRISON

ABSTRACT. We collate information about the fusion categories with  $A_n$  fusion rules. This note includes the classification of these categories, a realisation via the Temperley-Lieb categories, the auto-equivalence groups (both braided and tensor), identifications of the subcategories of invertible objects, and explicit descriptions of the Drinfeld centres.

The first section describes the classification of these categories (as monoidal, dagger, pivotal, and braided categories). The second section describes the properties of these categories.

# 1. CLASSIFICATION

We say that a fusion category has fusion rules  $A_{k+1}$  if there are k+1 isomorphism classes of simple objects, which we may index  $X_i$  for  $i=0,\ldots,k$ , such that:

- $X_0$  is the tensor identity,
- $X_i \otimes X_1 \cong X_{i-1} \oplus X_{i+1}$  when  $1 \le i < k$ ,
- $X_k \otimes X_1 \cong X_{k-1}$ .

The fusion graph for tensoring by  $X_1$  is thus the  $A_{k+1}$  Dynkin diagram:

$$X_0$$
  $X_1$   $X_2$   $X_{k-1}$   $X_k$ 

These hypotheses then ensure that the fusion rules are commutative, and in particular that

$$X_{i} \otimes X_{j} \cong \begin{cases} X_{|i-j|} \oplus X_{|i-j|+2} \oplus \cdots \oplus X_{i+j} & i+j \leq k \\ X_{|i-j|} \oplus X_{|i-j|+2} \oplus \cdots \oplus X_{2k-(i+j)} & i+j > k. \end{cases}$$

The only category with  $A_1$  fusion rules is Vec. For all  $k \ge 1$ , the monoidal categories with fusion rules  $A_{k+1}$  are classified by  $m \in \mathbb{Z}/(k+2)\mathbb{Z}$  with (m,k+2)=1. See [FK93] for details. We name these monoidal categories  $C_{k,m}$ . The monoidal category  $C_{k,m}$  has minimal field of definition  $\mathbb{Q}(q)$  where  $q=e^{\frac{m\pi i}{k+2}}$ . The value  $q+q^{-1}$  is a complete invariant of the monoidal categories with  $A_{k+1}$  fusion rules.

**Dagger structures.** The monoidal category  $C_{k,m}$  admits a dagger structure  $\dagger$  given by the conjugate linear extension of reflection in a horizontal line. All other dagger structures are of the form  $\phi \mapsto \lambda^{(n-m)/2} \phi^{\dagger}$  (where  $\phi : \text{Hom}(X^{\otimes n} \to X^{\otimes m})$ ) for some  $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{\times}$ . We denote this dagger category  $C_{k,m,\lambda}^{\dagger}$ .

**Braidings.** We recall that for k > 1, the braided fusion categories with fusion rules  $A_{k+1}$  are classified by  $\ell \in \mathbb{Z}/4(k+2)\mathbb{Z}$  with  $(\ell, k+2) = 1$ . See [FK93, Proposition 8.2.6] for details. We name these braided categories  $C_{k,\ell}^{\text{br}}$ . We can recover the underlying monoidal category of  $C_{k,\ell}^{\text{br}}$  by taking  $m := \ell \pmod{k+2}$ .

The braided category  $C_{k,l}^{\text{br}}$  has a split rational form over  $\mathbb{Q}(s)$ , where  $s = \exp\left(2\pi i \frac{\ell + k + 2}{4(k + 2)}\right) = i \exp\left(\pi i \frac{\ell}{2(k + 2)}\right)$ . We have  $s^2 = \pm q$ , and this s is a complete invariant of the braided category.

The above classification does not hold for k = 1 as it double counts the braided fusion categories. The 4 braided fusion categories with the  $A_2$  fusion rules are usually called Rep( $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ ), sVec, Sem, and Sem. (The category Sem is the "semions", and Sem its Galois conjugate.) The  $A_2$  categories can be identified as:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Rep}(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}) &= C_{1,7}^{\text{br}} = C_{1,11}^{\text{br}} \\ \text{sVec} &= C_{1,1}^{\text{br}} = C_{1,5}^{\text{br}} \\ \text{Sem} &= C_{1,4}^{\text{br}} = C_{1,8}^{\text{br}} \\ \overline{\text{Sem}} &= C_{1,2}^{\text{br}} = C_{1,10}^{\text{br}}. \end{aligned}$$

**Monoidal equivalence.** Recall that the braided category  $C_{k,\ell}^{\text{br}}$  has underlying monoidal category  $C_{k,\ell}^{\text{lood }k+2)}$ . Thus the braided category  $C_{k,\ell_1}^{\text{br}}$  is monoidally equivalent to  $C_{k,\ell_2}^{\text{br}}$  if and only if  $\ell_1 \equiv \pm \ell_2 \pmod{2(k+2)}$ . When k > 1 each monoidal category admits 4 distinct braided structures, and when k = 1 each monoidal category admits 2 distinct braided structures.

**Pivotality.** The monoidal categories  $C_{k,m}$  are rigid, with  $Y^* = Y$  for every object Y, and the coevaluation and evaluation maps are the cups and caps.

The monoidal categories  $C_{k,m}$  have the rather uninteresting pivotal structure given by the identity morphism on X, thought of as an isomorphism  $X \cong X^{**} = X$ . As  $C_{k,m}$  has universal grading group  $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$  there exist precisely two pivotal structures [DGNO10]. The other pivotal structure on  $C_{k,m}$  is given by minus the identity on X. We call these two pivotal categories  $C_{k,m,+}$  and  $C_{k,m,-}$  respectively.

**Galois conjugates.** The monoidal categories  $C_{k,m}$  form two Galois equivalence classes when k is odd, depending on the parity of m. When k is even the monoidal categories lie in a single Galois orbit. The braided categories  $C_{k,\ell}^{\text{br}}$  form three Galois equivalence classes when k is even, depending on whether  $\ell$  (mod 4) lies in  $\{0\}$ ,  $\{2\}$ , or  $\{1,3\}$ . When k is even the braided categories lie in a single Galois orbit.

**Sphericality.** The pivotal categories  $C_{k,m,\pm}$  are always spherical.

**Modularity.** When k is odd and  $2 \nmid \ell$  the rank of the S-matrix of  $C_{k,\ell,\pm}^{br}$  is  $\frac{k+1}{2}$ . For all the other cases the S-matrix has full rank and hence the categories are modular.

The conductor of  $C_{k,\ell,\pm}^{\rm br}$  (the order of the *T*-matrix ) depends on  $k+\ell\pmod 4$ . When  $k\neq 1$  the conductor is as follows:

| $k + \ell \pmod{4}$ | Conductor |
|---------------------|-----------|
| 0                   | k+2       |
| 1                   | 4(k+2)    |
| 2                   | 2(k+2)    |
| 3                   | 4(k+2)    |

In particular when k is even the conductor is always 4(k + 2).

The conductors of Rep( $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ ), sVec, Sem, and  $\overline{\text{Sem}}$  are 1,2,4, and 4 respectively.

**Drinfeld centre.** The monoidal category  $C_{k,m}$  can be equiped with a braiding to give the braided category  $C_{k,m}^{\text{br}}$ . This braided category is modular when k is even or  $2 \mid m$ . For these cases we can use the results of [Müg03] to see that the Drinfeld centre of the monoidal category  $C_{k,m}$  is the braided category  $C_{k,m}^{\text{br}} \boxtimes C_{k,m}^{\text{br op}}$ . When k is odd and  $2 \nmid m$  the monoidal category  $C_{k,m}$  does not admit a modular braiding, so we have to work harder to compute the centre. As monoidal categories we have an equivalence  $C_{k,m} \cong \text{Ad}(C_{k,m}) \boxtimes C_{1,1}$ . Thus  $Z(C_{k,m}) \cong Z(\text{Ad}(C_{k,m})) \boxtimes Z(C_{1,1})$  as the Drinfeld centre respects products of categories [KZ15].

The monoidal category  $\mathrm{Ad}(C_{k,m})$  admits a modular braiding, that is the braiding inherited from the braided category  $C_{k,m}^{\mathrm{br}}$ . Thus  $Z(\mathrm{Ad}(C_{k,m}))\cong\mathrm{Ad}(C_{k,m}^{\mathrm{br}})\boxtimes\mathrm{Ad}(C_{k,m}^{\mathrm{br}})$ .

The monoidal category  $C_{1,1}$  is better known as  $\text{Vec}(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})$ . The Drinfeld centre of this monoidal category is described in [Ost03b] and a skein theoretic presentation is given in [EM17].
