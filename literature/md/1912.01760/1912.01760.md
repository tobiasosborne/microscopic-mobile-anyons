#### **Abstract**

This is the second part of a two-part work on the unified mathematical theory of gapped and gapless edges of 2+1D topological orders. In Part I, we have developed the mathematical theory of chiral gapless edges. In Part II, we study boundary-bulk relation and non-chiral gapless edges. In particular, we explain how the notion of the center of an enriched monoidal category naturally emerges from the boundary-bulk relation. After the study of 0+1D gapless walls, we give the complete boundary-bulk relation for 2+1D topological orders with chiral gapless edges (including gapped edges) and 0d walls between edges. This relation is stated precisely and proved rigorously as a monoidal equivalence, which generalizes the functoriality of the usual Drinfeld center to an enriched setting. We also develop the mathematical theory of non-chiral gapless edges and 0+1D walls, and explain how to gap out certain non-chiral 1+1D gapless edges and 0+1D gapless walls categorically. In the end, we show that all anomalyfree 1+1D boundary-bulk rational CFT's can be recovered from 2d topological orders with chiral gapless edges via a dimensional reduction process. This provides physical meanings to some mysterious connections between mathematical results in fusion categories and those in rational CFT's.

# <span id="page-1-0"></span>1 Introduction

Throughout this paper, we use nd to denote the spatial dimension and n+1D to denote the spacetime dimension, and we use **Theorem**<sup>ph</sup> to highlight a physical result and use **Theorem** to represent a mathematically rigorous result.

This work is a continuation of [KZ4], in which we have developed the mathematical theory of chiral gapless edges of 2d topological orders (without symmetries). Its main result is summarized by the following physical theorem.

**Theorem**<sup>ph</sup> **1.1** ([KZ4]). 1d chiral gapped/gapless edges of an anomaly-free 2d topological order  $(\mathfrak{C}, c)$  is mathematically described and classified by pairs  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{B}}\mathfrak{X})$ , where

- 1. V is the chiral symmetry, i.e. a unitary rational VOA such that the category  $\mathcal{B} := \text{Mod}_V$  of V-modules is a UMTC. When  $V = \mathbb{C}$ , the edge is gapped and  $\mathcal{B} = \mathbf{H}$ .
- 2.  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}$  is a  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched unitary fusion category canonically constructed from the pair  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{X})$ , where  $\mathcal{X}$  is a UFC and a left fusion  $\mathcal{B}$ -module (see Definition 2.3), and  $\mathcal{X}$  is the underlying category of the enriched category  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}$ . More explicitly,
  - (a) objects  $x, y, z \in {}^{\mathbb{B}}\mathfrak{X}$  are precisely those in  $\mathfrak{X}$ , and are topological edge excitations;
  - (b) the morphism spaces are given by the internal homs, i.e.  $hom_{\mathfrak{B}_{\mathfrak{X}}}(x,y) := [x,y]_{\mathfrak{B}}$ .

Moreover, we have a direct sum decomposation  ${}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X} = {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X}_n$ , where each indecomposable direct summand  $\mathcal{X}_i$  of  $\mathcal{X}$  is the category of boundary conditions of a modular-invariant bulk CFT  $A_{\text{bulk}}^{(i)}$ . For  $x,y \in \mathcal{X}_i$ ,  $[x,x]_{\mathcal{B}}$  and  $[y,y]_{\mathcal{B}}$  are boundary CFT's of  $A_{\text{bulk}}^{(i)}$  and  $[x,y]_{\mathcal{B}}$  is a 0D wall between them. The bulk CFT's  $A_{\text{bulk}}^{(i)}$  and  $A_{\text{bulk}}^{(j)}$  are potentially different for  $i \neq j$ . The space  $[x,y]_{\mathcal{B}}$  should also be viewed as the space of instantons bewteen two edge excitations x and y. When  $V=\mathbb{C}$ ,  $\mathcal{X}=(\mathbb{C},{}^{\mathbf{H}}\mathcal{X})$  is a gapped edge.

One of the consequences of above theorem is that all 1d chiral gapless edges are obtained from *topological Wick rotations* [\[KZ4,](#page-53-0) Section 5.2]. It is physically absurd if this result does not generalize to 0d walls between edges. Inspired by this observation, we propose the following correspondence, which plays the role of a guiding principle of this work.

**Gapped-gapless Correspondence**: All gapless edges and 0d walls between edges of 2d topological orders can be obtained from topological Wick rotations plus the information of local quantum (i.e. chiral or non-chiral) symmetries.

Actually, some parts of our analysis do not use this principle but leads to results respecting this principle. At the end of the day, all 0d walls respect this principle. This is a low dimensional case of a more general principle for gapless phases in all dimensions proposed in [\[KZ4,](#page-53-0) Section 7]. It provides a powerful tool and a guiding principle for the study of gapless phases in all dimensions (see Section [7\)](#page-49-0).

In this work, we develop the mathematical theory of 0+1D walls between two gapless edges, boundary-bulk relation including 0+1D walls and that of non-chiral gapless edges. The logic flow and the layout of this work are given below.

In Section [2,](#page-3-0) we review the boundary-bulk relation for gapped edges. In particular, in Section [2.1,](#page-3-1) we review some basic mathematical notions, such as a closed module over a multifusion category and a closed monoidal modules over a braided fusion category. In Section [2.2,](#page-4-0) we review the mathematical theory of 0d wall between gapped edges. In Section [2.3,](#page-6-0) we review the boundary-bulk relation for gapped edges including 0d walls. In Section [2.4,](#page-7-0) we review the theory of generalized 0d defects and that of factorization homology. The anomaly-free condition discussed there will be used in many places later.

In Section [3,](#page-8-0) we develop the mathematical theory of 0+1D walls between two chiral gapless edges. We start with a careful analysis of observables on the world line of a 0+1D wall in Section [3.1.](#page-9-0) This analysis shows that a natural construction of a 0+1D wall automatically respects the Gapped-gapless Correspondence. In Section [3.2,](#page-11-0) we discuss more general constructions all respecting the Gapped-gapless Correspondence. In particular, we show that a 1+1D chiral symmetry in a neighborhood of the world line and a 0+1D chiral symmetry on the world line are both needed as defining data. Since there is no thermodynamics in 0d, we carefully distinguish the spatial notion of a 0d wall and the spacetime notion of a 0+1D wall (see Definition [3.2\)](#page-13-0). All 0+1D walls are spatially equivalent to the unique 0d wall. In Section [3.3,](#page-14-0) using Gappedgapless Correspondence, we conclude that we have found the mathematical description and the classification of all 0+1D gapless walls between two chiral gapless edges. In Section [3.4,](#page-16-0) we discuss how to fuse two 0+1D walls along a spatial direction and an anomaly associated to it, called spatial fusion anomaly. In Section [3.5,](#page-18-0) we show that the spatial equivalence between two 0+1D walls leads to a mathematical notion of a spatial equivalence between bimodules over enriched multi-fusion categories and the associated spatial Morita theory. As a consequence, two chiral gapless edges are spatially Morita equivalent if and only if they share the same bulk, and the spatial Morita equivalence is precisely defined by a 0+1D gapless wall as a spatially invertible bimodule.

After the preparation in Section [3,](#page-8-0) we are ready to give a complete boundary-bulk relation for chiral gapless edges in Section [4.](#page-22-0) We warm up to the precise statement by first explaining how the notion of the center of an enriched monoidal category naturally emerges from the physical intuition of the relation between a 2d bulk and a 1d edge in Section [4.1](#page-22-1) and [4.2.](#page-24-0) In Section [4.3,](#page-26-0) we add 0+1D walls to the edge and 1+1D gapless walls to the bulk. In Section [4.4,](#page-28-0) we give our main mathematical result (see Theorem [4.15\)](#page-29-2). It says that assigning the data on the boundary to that in the bulk by taking centers gives a well-defined functor, which is actually a monoidal equivalence. This generalizes our earlier result of the functoriality of Drinfeld center in [\[KZ1,](#page-52-0) Theorem 3.3.7].

In Section 5, we develop the mathematical theory of non-chiral gapless edges. The logic flow there is parallel to that of chiral gapless edges. In particular, we provide a classification of non-chiral gapless edges in Section 5.2, and discuss its significance in the study of purely edge topological phase transitions in Section 5.3. Different from the chiral cases, two non-chiral gapless edges can have very complicated 0+1D gapless walls. Mathematically, this corresponds to the representation theories of non-chiral symmetries in different categories. We explain this in Section 5.4.

In Section 6, we show how to use our theory to compute various physical processes. In particular, in Section 6.1, we show how to gap out a non-chiral 0+1D walls; in Section 6.2, we show how to fuse two gapless holes in a 2d topological order; in Section 6.3, we show how to recover all 1+1D anomaly-free boundary-bulk CFT's via a dimensional reduction process. At the same time, we clarify some mysterious connections between mathematical results in fusion categories and those in rational CFT's.

In Section 7, we discuss two important lessons we have learned from this work. These lessons are important to the future study of higher dimensional gapped/gapless phases.

# <span id="page-3-0"></span>2 Boundary-bulk relation I: gapped edges

In this section, we review some basic mathematical notions and boundary-bulk relation for gapped edges of 2d topological orders.

# <span id="page-3-1"></span>2.1 Basics of braided fusion categories

For a unitary multi-fusion category (UMFC)  $\mathcal{C}$ , we denote its tensor product by  $\otimes$ , its tensor unit by  $\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{C}}$  and the identity morphisms by  $\mathbf{1}_x: x \to x$  for  $x \in \mathcal{C}$ . A UMFC is called indecomposable if it is a not direct sum of two non-zero UMFC's. A unitary fusion category (UFC) is a UMFC with a simple tensor unit. We use  $\mathcal{C}^{\text{rev}}$  to denote the same category as  $\mathcal{C}$  but equipped with the tensor product  $\otimes^{\text{rev}}$  defined by  $a \otimes^{\text{rev}} b := b \otimes a$ ; and use  $\mathcal{C}^{\text{op}}$  to denotes the opposite category. The simplest UFC is the category  $\mathbf{H}$  of finite dimensional Hilbert spaces. Deligne tensor product is denoted by  $\mathbf{Z}$ .

**Definition 2.1.** For UMFC's  $\mathcal{B}$  and  $\mathcal{C}$ , a left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module is a finite unitary category  $\mathcal{M}$  equipped with a unitary monoidal functor

$$\phi_{\mathcal{M}}: \mathcal{B} \to \operatorname{Fun}(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{M}),$$
 (2.1)

where Fun( $\mathfrak{M},\mathfrak{M}$ ) denotes the category of unitary functors from  $\mathfrak{M}$  to  $\mathfrak{M}$ ; a right  $\mathfrak{C}$ -module is a left  $\mathfrak{C}^{rev}$ -module; a  $\mathfrak{B}$ - $\mathfrak{C}$ -bimodule is a left  $\mathfrak{B} \boxtimes \mathfrak{C}^{rev}$ -module. A (left, right, bi-)module is called *closed* if  $\phi_{\mathfrak{M}}$  is also an equivalence.

**Remark 2.2.** For a left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module module, for  $b \in \mathcal{B}$ , we often denote the endo-functor  $\phi_{\mathcal{M}}(b)$ :  $\mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}$  by  $b \odot -$ , where  $\odot : \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}$  is a well-defined  $\mathcal{B}$ -action on  $\mathcal{M}$ . Two  $\mathcal{B}$ -modules  $\mathcal{M}$  and  $\mathcal{N}$  are equivalent if there exists an equivalence between  $\mathcal{M}$  and  $\mathcal{N}$  intertwining the  $\mathcal{B}$ -actions.

For UMFC's  $\mathcal{A}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$ ,  $\mathcal{C}$ , an  $\mathcal{A}$ - $\mathcal{B}$ -bimodule  $\mathcal{M}$  and a  $\mathcal{B}$ - $\mathcal{C}$ -bimodule  $\mathcal{N}$ , the relative tensor product  $\mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}} \mathcal{N}$  is a well-defined  $\mathcal{A}$ - $\mathcal{C}$ -bimodule. We have a well-defined symmetric mononoidal category:

• ind UMF: objects are indecomposable UMFC's; morphisms are the equivalence classes of bimodules; the composition maps are defined by relative tensor products; the symmetric tensor product is the Deligne tensor product.

For a UMTC  $\mathbb{C}$ , we use  $\overline{\mathbb{C}}$  to denote the same unitary fusion category but with the braidings defined by the anti-braidings of  $\mathbb{C}$ .

<span id="page-4-1"></span>**Definition 2.3** ([KZ1]). For UMTC's  $\mathcal{B}$  and  $\mathcal{C}$ , a (multi-)fusion right  $\mathcal{C}$ -module  $\mathcal{M}$  is a UFC (or UMFC) equipped with a unitary braided monoidal functor  $\phi_{\mathcal{M}}:\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{J}(\mathcal{M})$ ; a (multi-)fusion left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module is a (multi-)fusion right  $\overline{\mathcal{B}}$ -module; a (multi-)fusion  $\mathcal{B}$ - $\mathcal{C}$ -bimodule is a (multi-)fusion right  $\overline{\mathcal{B}}$   $\otimes$   $\mathcal{C}$ -module. Such a (left, right, bi-)module is called *closed* if  $\phi_{\mathcal{M}}$  is also an equivalence.

Given a multi-fusion right  $\mathcal{C}$ -module  $\mathcal{M}$ , by composing  $\phi_{\mathcal{M}}$  with the forgetful functor  $\mathbf{f}$ :  $\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{M}) \to \mathcal{M}$ , we obtain the following commutative diagram:

$$\begin{array}{c}
C \xrightarrow{\phi_{\mathcal{M}}} 3(\mathcal{M}) \\
f_{\mathcal{M}} = \mathbf{f} \circ \phi_{\mathcal{M}} & f \\
\mathcal{M}
\end{array}$$

A functor  $\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{M}$  factoring through the forgetful functor **f** is called a central functor. The action functor  $\mathbb{C} : \mathbb{C} \times \mathbb{M} \to \mathbb{M}$  defined by  $(a, m) \mapsto f_{\mathbb{M}}(a) \otimes m$  is a monoidal functor.

<span id="page-4-3"></span>**Definition 2.4.** For a UMFC  $\mathcal{C}$  and two right multi-fusion  $\mathcal{C}$ -modules  $\mathcal{M}$ ,  $\mathcal{N}$ , a monoidal  $\mathcal{C}$ -module functor  $F: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{N}$  is a unitary monoidal functor equipped with an isomorphism of monoidal functors  $F \circ f_{\mathcal{M}} \simeq f_{\mathcal{N}} : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{N}$  rendering the following diagram

$$F(f_{\mathcal{M}}(a) \otimes x) \xrightarrow{\sim} F(x \otimes f_{\mathcal{M}}(a))$$

$$\downarrow \qquad \qquad \qquad \downarrow \sim$$

$$f_{\mathcal{N}}(a) \otimes F(x) \xrightarrow{\sim} F(x) \otimes f_{\mathcal{N}}(a)$$

$$(2.2)$$

commutative for  $a \in \mathbb{C}$  and  $x \in \mathbb{M}$ .  $\mathbb{M}$  and  $\mathbb{N}$  are said to be equivalent if F is also an equivalence.

For UMTC's  $\mathcal{A}$ ,  $\mathcal{B}$ ,  $\mathcal{C}$ , a multi-fusion  $\mathcal{A}$ - $\mathcal{B}$ -bimodule  $\mathcal{M}$  and a multi-fusion  $\mathcal{B}$ - $\mathcal{C}$ -module  $\mathcal{N}$ , the relative tensor product  $\mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}} \mathcal{N}$  is a well-defined multi-fusion  $\mathcal{A}$ - $\mathcal{C}$ -bimodule. By [KZ1, Theorem 3.3.6.]. we have a well-defined symmetric monoidal category:

• UMT<sup>cl</sup>: objects are UMTC's; morphisms are the equivalence classes of closed multi-fusion bimodules; the composition maps are defined by relative tensor products; the symmetric tensor product is the Deligne tensor product.

**Definition 2.5.** A  $\mathcal{B}$ -enriched unitary (multi-)fusion category is an enriched monoidal category  $\mathcal{B} \mathcal{X}$  obtained by the canonical construction from a pair  $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{X})$ , where  $\mathcal{B}$  is a UMTC and  $\mathcal{X}$  is a (multi-)fusion left  $\mathcal{B}$ -module.

#### <span id="page-4-0"></span>2.2 Gapped edges and 0d walls

An anomaly-free 2d topological order (without symmetry) can be described by a pair  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  (see [Ki1, Appendix E] for a review), where  $\mathcal{C}$  is a UMTC and c is the chiral central charge. The pair  $(\mathbf{H}, 0)$  describes the trivial 2d topological oder.

<span id="page-4-2"></span>**Theorem**<sup>ph</sup> **2.6.** As illustrated in Figure. 1, we have the following results.

<span id="page-5-0"></span>Figure 1: This picture depicts a 2d topological order (C, 0) with three different gapped edges given by UFC's L, M, N separated by two 0d walls X and Y. The 2d bulk is oriented as the usual **R** <sup>2</sup> with the normal direction pointing out of the paper in readers' direction. The arrows indicate the induced orientation on the edge.

- 1. A gapped edge of a 2d topological order (C, 0) is described mathematically by a closed right fusion C-module L.
- 2. Different gapped edges L, M, N (as UFC's) share the same bulk (as their Drinfeld centers) if and only if they are Morita equivalent [\[ENO2\]](#page-51-0).

A 2d topological order (C, 0) admitting gapped edges is called *a non-chiral 2d topological order*. In these cases, the central functor *f*<sup>L</sup> describes how excitations in the bulk are fused into those on the edge, thus will be called the bulk-to-boundary map.

**Remark 2.7.** Unstable 1d topological orders naturally occur in dimensional reduction processes. They can be described by an indecomposable UMFC [\[KWZ1,](#page-52-2) [AKZ\]](#page-51-1).

<span id="page-5-1"></span>**Remark 2.8.** By the folding trick, Theoremph [2.6](#page-4-2) implies that a gapped 1d wall between two 2d topological orders (A, *c*) and (B, *c*) (see the second picture in [\(2.5\)](#page-7-1)) is described by a closed fusion A-B-bimodule or a closed multi-fusion A-B-bimodule if we allow unstable gapped walls.

A 1d gapped edge L of a 2d topological order should itself be viewed as an anomalous 1d topological order, described mathematically by a UFC L. Its anomaly is completely captured by its bulk, which is described by the Drinfeld center Z(L). It is anomaly-free if Z(L) ' **H**.

<span id="page-5-2"></span>**Theorem**ph **2.9** ([\[KWZ1,](#page-52-2) [AKZ\]](#page-51-1))**.** A 0d wall between two gapped edges L and M (i.e. UFC's) of the same 2d topological order (C, *c*) as depicted in Figure [1](#page-5-0) is mathematically described by the unique closed left L -<sup>C</sup> Mrev-bimodule X.

**Remark 2.10.** Physically, the L-M-bimodule structure on X is provided by the fusion of topological excitations in L and M to X from two sides. The closedness condition is an anomaly-free condition, which says that the 1d topological order L -<sup>C</sup> Mrev, obtained from the dimensional reduction process depicted in Figure [2](#page-6-1) [\[FSV,](#page-51-2) [Ko3\]](#page-52-3), should be nothing but the unique 1d bulk of X given by Fun(X, X) (see [\[KWZ1,](#page-52-2) [AKZ\]](#page-51-1) for more details). This condition determines X uniquely (up to equivalences).

If we consider the entire 0+1D world line of the 0d wall, it makes no sense to specify a wall excitation *x* ∈ X because it can be changed to other excitations on the world line. But if we want to specify a particular spatial slide of the 0+1D wall, we can further specify a distinguished wall excitation *x* ∈ X. This leads to a new description of the 0d wall as a pair (X, *x*), which is useful in the calculation of global observables or factorization homology on space manifolds (see Section [2.4\)](#page-7-0).

**Remark 2.11.** Mathematically, such a pair (X, *x*) can be viewed as an *E*0-algebra in the 2-category of categories in the sense of Lurie [\[Lu\]](#page-53-1). In the same 2-category, a monoidal category is an

# <span id="page-6-0"></span>**2.3 Boundary-bulk relation for gapped edges**

It turns out that the boundary-bulk relation discussed in the previous subsections is only the first layer of a heirarchic structure.

A most general situation for the boundary-bulk relation is depicted in Figure [3.](#page-7-3) The 0d gapped defect labeled by X is a junction of three 1d gapped defects labeled by L, M,Z (1)(X). In this case, X is an L-M-bimodule but not invertible in general. The 1d gapped wall labeled by Z (1)(X) is a closed multi-fusion Z(L)-Z(M)-bimodule (recall Remark [2.8\)](#page-5-1). By the unique bulk principle proposed in [\[KWZ1\]](#page-52-2), the gapped 1d wall Z (1)(X), which should be viewed as a 1d "relative bulk" of X, is uniquely determined by X, L, M as follows:

$$\mathfrak{Z}^{(1)}(\mathfrak{X}) = \operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{L}|\mathcal{M}}(\mathfrak{X}, \mathfrak{X}),$$

where FunL|M(X, X) is the category of unitary L-M-bimodule functors. Moreover, we should have a canonical monoidal equivalence:

$$\mathcal{L} \boxtimes_{\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{L})} \mathfrak{Z}^{(1)}(\mathfrak{X}) \boxtimes_{\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{M})} \mathfrak{M}^{\text{rev}} \simeq \text{Fun}(\mathfrak{X}, \mathfrak{X})$$

which is a consequence of the formula [\(2.4\)](#page-7-2).

**Remark 2.13.** Note that our convention of the left and right action in the definition of a fusion bimodule is that if the orientation of the wall is the same (resp. the opposite) as the induced orientation with respect to a bulk phase, then this bulk phase acts on the wall from right (resp. left). We will use this convention throughout this work.

<span id="page-7-3"></span>Figure 3: The picture illustrates the complete boundary-bulk relation, which is the physical meaning of Theorem [2.14](#page-7-4) . The arrows indicate the orientation of the edges/walls and the order of the fusion product of topological excitations on the edges/walls.

Now we consider the fusion of two gapped walls, say Z (1)(X) and Z (1)(Y). This fusion gives a new gapped wall Z (1)(X) -<sup>Z</sup>(B) Z (1)(Y) between Z(A) and Z(C). On the other hand, it should also be viewed as the 1d "relative bulk" of a new 0d wall between A and C obtained by fusing X and Y, i.e. X -<sup>M</sup> Y. Hence, we should expect a monoidal equivalence:

<span id="page-7-2"></span>
$$\operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{L}|\mathcal{M}}(\mathcal{X},\mathcal{X})\boxtimes_{\mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{M})}\operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{M}|\mathcal{N}}(\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{Y})\simeq\operatorname{Fun}_{\mathcal{L}|\mathcal{N}}(\mathcal{X}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{M}}\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{X}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{M}}\mathcal{Y}). \tag{2.4}$$

This monoidal equivalence was rigorously proved in [\[KZ1,](#page-52-0) Theorem 3.1.7.]. It simply says that the assignment L 7→ Z(L) and X 7→ Z (1)(X) is functorial. $$\mathcal{L} \mapsto \mathfrak{Z}(\mathcal{L})$$

# <span id="page-7-0"></span>**2.4 Factorization homology on space manifolds**

The integration of the local observables on space manifolds is achieved by the mathematical theory of factorization homology [\[AKZ\]](#page-51-1) (see [\[AF\]](#page-51-3) for a recent mathematical review). In this subsection, we review some basic results that will be useful later.

<span id="page-7-5"></span>**Definition 2.16.** A coefficient system *A* of an oriented disk-stratified 2-manifold Σ (see [\[AFT\]](#page-51-4)) is an assignment of each *i*-cell to an *i*-dimensional topological order for *i* = 0, 1, 2. A coefficient system *A* is called *anomaly-free* if the following conditions are satisfied:

<span id="page-7-1"></span>

- 1. each 2-cell is assigned to a UMTC  $\mathcal{A}$  (or an anomaly-free 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{A}, c)$ , where c is fixed for each connecting component of  $\Sigma$ , thus can be ignored);
- 2. each oriented 1-cell between two adjacent 2-cells (as illustrated in the second picture in (2.5)) is assigned to a closed multi-fusion A-B-bimodule M;
- 3. each 0-cell as the one depicted in the third picture in (2.5) is assigned to a pair  $(\mathfrak{X}, x)$ , where  $\mathfrak{X}$  is a closed  $\mathfrak{P}$ -module for  $\mathfrak{P} := \mathfrak{M}_1 \boxtimes_{\overline{\mathcal{A}_0} \boxtimes \mathcal{A}_1} (\mathfrak{M}_2 \boxtimes_{\mathcal{A}_2} \cdots \boxtimes_{\mathcal{A}_{n-1}} \mathfrak{M}_n)$  (recall Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 2.9) and x is an object in  $\mathfrak{X}$ .

Such a coefficient system describes a physical configuration of 0d, 1d, 2d topological orders on  $\Sigma$ . Anomaly-free condition means that the corresponding physical configuration can be realized by a 2-dimensional local Hamiltonian lattice model on  $\Sigma$ . The "closed" condition determines  $\mathfrak X$  uniquely. In other words, an anomaly-free 0d defect is determined by the physics of its neighborhood uniquely (up to the choices of the distinguished object  $x \in \mathfrak X$ ).

**Remark 2.17.** Note that if we flip the orientation of a 1-cell and replaced its assignment  $\mathfrak{M}$  by  $\mathfrak{M}^{rev}$  at the same time, then the physics configuration remains the same. Therefore, it must defines an equivalent coefficient system.

The factorization homology of a coefficient system A on an oriented disk-stratified 2-manifold  $\Sigma$  is well-defined and is denoted by  $\int_{\Sigma} A$ .

**Theorem 2.18** ([AKZ]). If  $\Sigma$  is compact and A is anomaly-free, then  $\int_{\Sigma} A = (\mathbf{H}, u_{\Sigma})$ , where  $u_{\Sigma}$  is a distinguished object in  $\mathbf{H}$ .

The physical meaning of  $u_{\Sigma}$  is nothing but the space of ground states of the associated physical configuration on  $\Sigma$ . This integral is well-defined on any submanifold of  $\Sigma$  as well. In particular, the integral over any open 2-disk like region D in  $\Sigma$  gives a pair  $(\mathfrak{X},x)$ , i.e.  $\int_D A = (\mathfrak{X},x)$ . The following result will be useful later.

<span id="page-8-1"></span>**Theorem 2.19** ([AKZ]). By shrinking an open 2-disk like region D in  $\Sigma$  to a 0-cell and assigning  $\int_D A$  to this 0-cell, we obtain a coefficient system A' on a new oriented disk-stratified 2-manifold  $\Sigma'$ . The coefficient system A' on  $\Sigma'$  is again anomaly-free.

Do different background categories  $\mathcal{P}_{X|X}$  and  $\mathcal{P}$  produce different 0d walls? For  $Q:=\mathcal{P}_{X|X}$  and an invertible  $\mathfrak{Q}$ - $\mathfrak{P}$ -bimodule  $\mathfrak{K}:=\mathcal{P}_{X|\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{P}}}$ , we obtain a 0d wall defined by  $(V,{}^{\mathfrak{Q}}(\mathcal{K}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}}\mathcal{M}))$  as illustrated in Figure 5, in which  $\mathcal{K}$  is depicted very close to  $\mathcal{M}$  such that  $\mathcal{K}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}}\mathcal{M}$  should be viewed as a single fictional defect junction in the figure and the category of 0d wall excitations. We want to compare  $(V,{}^{\mathfrak{Q}}(\mathcal{K}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}}\mathcal{M}))$  with  $(V,{}^{\mathfrak{P}}\mathcal{M})$ .

- 1. We compare the boundary CFT's on the wall  $(V, {}^{\Omega}(X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} M))$  with those on  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{P}}M)$ . Note that  ${}^{\mathcal{P}}M = {}^{\mathcal{P}}M_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus {}^{\mathcal{P}}M_n$ , where  $M_i$  are the indecomposable components of M as a left  $\mathcal{P}$ -module. On the one hand, for  $0 \neq m \in \mathcal{M}_i$ , we have  $M_i \simeq \mathcal{P}_{[m,m]_{\mathcal{P}}}$  and  $[m,m]_{\mathcal{P}}$  is a boundary CFT on the 0+1D world sheet of  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{P}}M)$ . On the other hand,  $X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} M_i \simeq \mathcal{P}_{X[[m,m]_{\mathcal{P}}}$  is an indecomposable  $\Omega$ -module. For  $x \in X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} M_i$ ,  $[x,x]_{\Omega}$  is a boundary CFT on the 0+1D world sheet of  $(V, {}^{\Omega}(X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} M))$ . Regarding  $[x,x]_{\Omega}$  as an algebra in  $\mathcal{P}$  via the forgetful functor  $f: \mathcal{P}_{X|X} \to \mathcal{P}$ , one can easily show that  $[x,x]_{\Omega}$  and  $[m,m]_{\mathcal{P}}$  are Morita equivalent. Therefore, the set of boundary CFT's on  $(V, {}^{\Omega}(X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} M))$  is a subset of those on  $(V, {}^{\mathcal{P}}M)$ .
- 2. We compare the categories of wall excitations. They are obviously different. What causes this difference? Recall that the previous notion of the chiral symmetry *V* is a 1+1D notion. It is a VOA that is transparent in a neighborhood of the world line except at 0D defects. Mathematically, it just means that a VOA is a conformal analogue of an *E*<sub>2</sub>-algebra (or a 2-disk algebra) [Lu, AFT, AF]. On the 0+1D world line of the wall, we can impose a new 0+1D chiral symmetry, which is only transparent on the world line except at 0D defects, and is potentially different from the chiral symmetry *V*. This 0+1D chiral symmetry should be given by an observable algebra only defined on an open 1-disk. Mathematically, it is a conformal analogue of an *E*<sub>1</sub>-algebra. In our case, it is nothing but an OSVOA, or more precisely, a symmetric special †-Frobenius algebra *A* in 𝔭. For a fixed 0+1D chiral symmetry *A*, it is clear that a 0D defect living on the world line must be an *A-A*-bimodule.

For example, for  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{D}}\mathbb{M})$ , this 0+1D chiral symmetry is just  $V_{\mathcal{A}} \otimes_{V} V_{\mathcal{B}}$ ; for  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{Q}}(\mathfrak{K} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{P}} \mathfrak{M}))$ , it is X. Their difference in the category of wall excitations is due to the fact that larger 0+1D chiral symmetry allows fewer wall excitations and fewer morphisms between wall excitations. Moreover, Figure 5 shows that one can change the 0+1D chiral symmetry by introducing a 0D wall (e.g.  $\mathcal{K}$ ) on the world line.

- 3. Although  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{Q}}(\mathfrak{K} \boxtimes_{\mathfrak{P}} \mathfrak{M}))$  and  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{P}}\mathfrak{M})$  differ in their 0+1D chiral symmetry and wall excitations, this difference is superficial from the usual condensed matter physics point of view because there is no thermodynamics limit in 0d. It means that changing the 0+1D chiral symmetry, or equivalently, introducing 0D fictional defects (e.g.  $\mathfrak{K}$ ) onto the 0+1D world line, does not trigger a real space phase transition. From this point of view, a 0d wall or an anomalous 0d phase should automatically include all possible 0+1D chiral symmetries and 0D defects (e.g.  $\mathfrak{K}$ ) on the world line, and  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{Q}}(\mathfrak{K} \boxtimes_{\mathfrak{P}} \mathfrak{M}))$  with  $(V, {}^{\mathfrak{P}}\mathfrak{M})$  should be viewed as two gauge equivalent descriptions of the same 0d wall.
- 4. Consider gapped 0d walls between two 1d gapped edges. In Theorem<sup>ph</sup> 2.9, the background category is fixed to **H**, as a consequence, the category of 0d wall excitations is unique. As we will show in Section 6.1, in certain length scale, not only it makes sense to talk about "a gappable gapless 0d wall", but also it has a precise mathematical description. Gapping it out does not trigger any 0d phase transition because there is no thermodynamics limit in 0d. Since there are precise mathematical descriptions before and after the gapping-out process, it is useful to introduce a notion before the gapping and a notion of "gauge equivalence" between the description of a 0d gapped wall and that of a gappable 0d gapless wall.

**Remark 3.1.** 1+1D and 0+1D chiral symmetries are both local quantum symmetries. We believe that their relation presented here catches some general features of gapless phases of all dimensions (see Section 7).

Similar phenomena also occur if we vary the chiral symmetry V. In general, there are more than one VOA V rendering Diagram (3.1) commutative. It means that we can impose different 1+1D chiral symmetries on the 0d wall. We denote them by  $V_i$ ,  $i=1,2,\cdots$ . We obtain different pairs  $(V_i, {}^{\mathcal{P}_i}\mathcal{M}_i)$ , where  $\mathcal{P}_i = (\mathrm{Mod}_{V_i})_{V_{\mathcal{A}}|V_{\mathcal{B}}}$  and  $\mathcal{M}_i$  is uniquely determined. By the construction of  $\mathcal{P}$  (recall (3.6)), it is clear that  $\mathcal{P}_i$  is again a closed multi-fusion  $\mathcal{A}$ - $\mathcal{B}$ -bimodules. Hence,  $\mathcal{P}_i$  and  $\mathcal{P}_j$  are Morita equivalent. By introducing a 0D wall  $\mathcal{K}_{ji}$  on the world line between  $\mathcal{P}_j$  and  $\mathcal{P}_i$ , we break/change the chiral symmetry from  $V_i$  to  $V_j$ . Due to the lack of thermodynamics limit in 0d, this breaking/changing of 1+1D chiral symmetries does not trigger a real space phase transition.

Note that the usual notion of a phase in condensed matter physics is a spatial notion. From this perspective, all possible  $(V_i, {}^{\mathcal{P}_i}\mathcal{M}_i)$  and 0D walls among them should be included in the complete definition of the spatial notion of a 0d phase. On the other hand,  $(V_i, {}^{\mathcal{P}_i}\mathcal{M}_i)$  and  $(V_j, {}^{\mathcal{P}_i}\mathcal{M}_j)$  for  $i \neq j$  define two different sets of boundary CFT's preserving different 0+1D chiral symmetries, and can be obtained from two different topological Wick rotations. It becomes convenient, or physically important, to introduce and carefully distinguish two concepts: a 0d phase (a spatial notion) and a 0+1D phase (a spacetime notion).

<span id="page-13-0"></span>**Definition 3.2.** There are two different notions associated to a gapless 0d wall or a potentially anomalous 0d gapless phase.

- 1. By a (potentially anomalous) "0+1D phase", we mean a 0d defect in a physical system with a fixed 1+1D chiral (resp. non-chiral) symmetry *V* defined in a 1+1D neighborhood of the world line and a fixed 0+1D chiral (resp. non-chiral) symmetry *X* defined on the world line.
- 2. By a (potentially anomalous) "0d phase", we mean a 0d defect in a physical system such that all possible 1+1D (resp. 0+1D) chiral (or non-chiral) symmetries are realized in a

neighborhood of (resp. on) the world line. More precisely, two 0+1D phases are called *spatially equivalent* if they can be transformed from one to the other by introducing a 0D defect (e.g. K in Figure [5\)](#page-12-0) on the world line. Then a 0d phase (or wall) is just a spatial equivalence class of 0+1D phases (or walls).

In the context of this subsection, we can denote the 0+1D walls constructed in this subsection by (*V*, *V*, <sup>P</sup>M), (*V*, *X*, <sup>Q</sup>(K -<sup>P</sup> M)) and (*V<sup>i</sup>* , *V<sup>i</sup>* , <sup>P</sup>*i*M*i*), respectively.

**Remark 3.3.** We will introduce the notion of a non-chiral symmetry in Section [5.2.](#page-32-0) Above definition also applies to the study of 0d walls between two non-chiral gapless edges (see Section [5.4\)](#page-36-0).

**Remark 3.4.** The spatial equivalence leads us to the mathematical notion of a spatial equivalence between two bimodules over enriched multi-fusion categories in Definition [3.22.](#page-20-0) In that context, <sup>P</sup>M and <sup>Q</sup>(K -<sup>P</sup> M) are spatially equivalent <sup>A</sup>X-<sup>B</sup>Y-bimodules.

**Remark 3.5.** The subtle difference between the spatial and spacetime notions in Definition [3.2](#page-13-0) is unique in 0d, and disappear in higher dimensions. For example, introducing a 1-codimensional wall on the 1+1D world sheet of a gapped or gapless edge triggers a real space phase transition.

<span id="page-14-1"></span>**Remark 3.6.** Actually, the spatial equivalence class of a 0+1D wall is much more than the 0+1D walls constructed in this subsection. For example, consider a 1+1D gapless phase defined by a RCFT defined on a cylinder *S* <sup>1</sup> × **R** 1 , where *S* 1 is the space manifold and **R** 1 is the time. Assume that the size of *S* 1 is small. Physically, we know that if we shrink *S* 1 to a point, the spectrum of the RCFT become gapped in this limit. Mathematically, by integrating the RCFT on the cylinder (via factorization homology), we obtain a mathematical description of a 0+1D wall, which is still gapless because this integration (or factorization homology) does not know the size of *S* 1 . But this wall is gappable. Its gappability can be characterized by spatial equivalences as we will show in Section [6.1.](#page-41-1) We denote it by (**C**,*Y*, S ), where **C** is the 1+1D local quantum symmetry and *Y* is the 0+1D non-chiral symmetry. By attaching this gappable 0+1D wall to any one of 0+1D chiral gapless walls constructed in this subsection, say (*V*, *V*, <sup>P</sup>M), we get a new 0+1D gapless wall (*V*, *V*, <sup>P</sup>M) - (**C**,*Y*, S ] ). This type of 0+1D gapless walls is beyond previous constructions. We would like to ignore such gappable 0+1D walls for our classification of 0+1D walls. Note that *Y* is infinite dimensional and does not live in **H**. By requiring the 0+1D chiral symmetry to be a symmetric special †-Frobenius algebra in (Mod*V*)*V*A|*V*<sup>B</sup> , we ensure that the 0+1D wall does not contain any gappable factors or parts. We will explain in details how to gap out a 0+1D gappable gapless wall in Section [6.1.](#page-41-1)

#### <span id="page-16-0"></span>3.4 Spatial fusion anomalies

In Figure 6, we depict three chiral gapless edges  $(V_i, {}^{\mathcal{B}_i}\mathfrak{X}_i)$ , i = 1, 2, 3 of a 2d topological order  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$ . They are connected by two 0d gapless walls  $(V_{12}, X, {}^{\mathcal{D}}\mathfrak{M})$  and  $(V_{23}, Y, {}^{\mathcal{D}}\mathfrak{N})$ . We would like to study the spatial fusion of these two gapless walls.

<span id="page-16-2"></span>**Remark 3.13.** According to the orientation of the edge, the spatial fusion is from right to left. It is against our usual convention of the order of tensor product unless we look at Figure 6 from the back. Using the following two canonical (monoidal) equivalences

$$\mathcal{P}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2}\mathcal{Q}\simeq\mathcal{Q}\boxtimes_{\overline{\mathcal{B}_2}}\mathcal{P};\qquad \mathcal{M}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\mathrm{rev}}}\mathcal{N}\simeq\mathcal{N}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2}\mathcal{M},$$

we can write the spatial fusion product from left to right (see (3.11)). Note that this convention is the opposite to the one we used in the gapped cases.

We first restrict ourselves to the special cases  $V = V_{12} = V_{23}$  and the spatial fusion preserves the 1+1D chiral symmetry V. Similar to the discussion in [KZ4, Section 6.3], the naive fusion of observables on two world lines:

$$[m,m']_{\mathcal{P}} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} [n,n']_{\mathcal{Q}} \in \mathcal{P} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} \mathcal{Q}$$

does not give the correct fusion in general. What happens is again a quantum quenching process. This fusion of two 0d walls causes the fusion between the topological excitations  $m \in \mathcal{M}$  and

those in  $n \in \mathbb{N}$  according to the following fusion functor:

$$\mathcal{M} \boxtimes \mathcal{N} \xrightarrow{\boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\text{rev}}}} \mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\text{rev}}} \mathcal{N}$$
$$m \boxtimes n \mapsto m \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\text{rev}}} n.$$

Therefore, the underlying category, or the category of topological excitations, of the resulting 0+1D wall is given by  $\mathbb{M} \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} \mathbb{N}$ . By the boundary-bulk relation (see Theorem 2.14), we obtain that the background category of the resulting 0+1D wall has to be the UMFC  $\mathcal{P} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} \mathcal{Q}$ . Fusing topological excitations  $m \in \mathcal{M}$  and  $n \in \mathbb{N}$  causes a change of the microscopic physics so that it is pushed away from an RG fixed point, but then must flow to a new RG fixed point. More precisely, when two excitations are getting close, certain non-local operators acting on two excitations are becoming local. As a consequence, local observables on the 0+1D world line supported on  $m \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n$  are more than the naive fusion of those on the world lines supported separately on m and n. According to the **Principle of Universality at RG fixed points**, introduced in [KZ4, Section 6.3], observables on the 0+1D world line supported on  $m \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n$  at the new RG fixed point must be the universal one, i.e. the internal hom  $[m \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n, m \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n] \in \mathcal{P} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} \mathcal{Q}$ . More generally, at the new RG fixed point, the space of boundary-condition changing operators between two boundary conditions  $m \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n$  and  $m' \boxtimes_{\chi_2^{\text{rev}}} n'$  is given by the internal hom:

$$[m\boxtimes_{\mathfrak{X}_2^{\mathrm{rev}}} n, m'\boxtimes_{\mathfrak{X}_2^{\mathrm{rev}}} n']\in \mathcal{P}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} \mathcal{Q}.$$

Therefore, in this case, we obtain the following fusion formula:

<span id="page-17-0"></span>
$$(V, X, {}^{\mathcal{D}}\mathcal{M}) \boxtimes_{(V_2, {}^{\mathcal{B}_2}\mathfrak{X}_2)} (V, Y, {}^{\mathcal{Q}}\mathcal{N}) := (V, X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y, {}^{(\mathcal{D}\boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2}\mathcal{Q})}(\mathcal{M} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\text{rev}}} \mathcal{N})), \tag{3.11}$$

where  $X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y$  is naturally a symmetric separable †-Frobenius algebra in  $(\operatorname{Mod}_V)_{V_1|V_3}$ , and we have a natural unitary monoidal equivalence  $\mathcal{P} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} \mathcal{Q} \simeq ((\operatorname{Mod}_V)_{V_1|V_3})_{X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y | X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y}$ .

It means that naive fusion of observables on the two world lines of two 0d gapless wall (i.e.  $[m,m']_{\mathcal{D}} \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} [n,n']_{\mathcal{Q}}$ ) is not universal or at RG fixed point. It will flow to a RG fixed point, which is universal and defined by  $[m \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2} n, m' \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2} n']$ . In some sense, this morphism f catches the information of the RG flow. Interestingly, even in the general cases, for a special class of edges excitations (or excitations in the trivial wall), f is an isomorphism (see [KZ4, Remark 6.3]).

From another point of view, that f is not an isomorphism simply can be viewed as an indicator that there are certain anomaly, called *spatial fusion anomalies*. Indeed, 1d gapless edges, together with 0d walls between them, are anomalous 1d phases when the bulk  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  is non-trivial. It is possible that this spatial fusion anomaly vanishes for some special anomalous phases as shown in the case discussed in the Eq. (3.13). But when the bulk phase  $(\mathcal{C}, c)$  is the trivial 2d topological order, the spatial fusion anomaly should definitely vanish. This is proved in [KYZ1, Theorem 4.5].

**Remark 3.15.** While spatial fusions are often anomalous, temporal fusions are often anomaly-free, i.e.  $[m', m'']_{\mathcal{P}} \boxtimes_{[m', m']_{\mathcal{P}}} [m, m']_{\mathcal{P}} \simeq [m, m'']_{\mathcal{P}}$  for simple  $m, m', m'' \in \mathcal{M}_i$  if all four internal homs are non-zero.

In general, the 1+1D chiral symmetries on two gapless walls  $V_{12}$  and  $V_{23}$  are potentially different, i.e.  $V_{12} \neq V_{23}$ . In this case, the spatial fusion of two walls causes the 1+1D chiral symmetries to break further down to a smaller VOA  $V_{123}$ . To compute the spatial fusion, we need further specify the fusion process. More precisely, we assume that this fusion is achieved in two steps: first breaking both 1+1D chiral symmetries  $V_{12}$  and  $V_{23}$  down to  $V_{123}$  without changing X and Y, then fusing according to (3.11). More explicitly, the first step gives:

$$(V_{12}, X, {}^{\mathcal{P}}\mathcal{M}) \mapsto (V_{123}, X, {}^{\mathcal{P}'}\mathcal{M}') \qquad (V_{23}, Y, {}^{\mathcal{Q}}\mathcal{N}) \mapsto (V_{123}, Y, {}^{\mathcal{Q}'}\mathcal{N}'),$$

where *X* and *Y* should be viewed as their images in  $(Mod_{V_{123}})_{V_1|V_2}$  via two forgetful functors:

$$(\text{Mod}_{V_{12}})_{V_1|V_2} \xrightarrow{\mathbf{f}} (\text{Mod}_{V_{123}})_{V_1|V_2} \xleftarrow{\mathbf{f}} (\text{Mod}_{V_{23}})_{V_1|V_2},$$

and  $\mathcal{P}' = ((\text{Mod}_{V_{123}})_{V_1|V_2})_{X|X}$  and  $\mathcal{Q}' = ((\text{Mod}_{V_{123}})_{V_1|V_2})_{Y|Y}$ , and  $\mathcal{M}', \mathcal{N}'$  are uniquely determined. The second step gives:

<span id="page-18-2"></span>
$$(V_{123}, X, {}^{\mathcal{P}'}\mathcal{M}') \boxtimes_{(V_2, {}^{\mathcal{B}_2}\mathcal{X}_2)} (V_{123}, Y, {}^{\mathcal{Q}'}\mathcal{N}') = (V_{123}, X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y, {}^{(\mathcal{P}'\boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2}\mathcal{Q}')}(\mathcal{M}' \boxtimes_{\mathcal{X}_2^{\text{rev}}} \mathcal{N}')). \tag{3.14}$$

where  $X \boxtimes_{\mathcal{B}_2} Y$  is naturally a symmetric separable †-Frobenius algebra in  $(\text{Mod}_{V_{123}})_{V_1|V_3}$ .

# <span id="page-22-0"></span>**4 Boundary-bulk relation II: chiral gapless edges**

In this section, we generalize the boundary-bulk relation for gapped edges to that for both gapped and chiral gapless edges.

# <span id="page-22-1"></span>**4.1 Bulk of a chiral gapless edge**

Given a chiral gapless edge (*V*, <sup>B</sup>X) of a bulk topological order (C, *c*), how to understand those bulk excitations in terms of those on the edge? Let us first look at the gapped cases. When the edge is gapped, i.e. *V* = **C**, B = **H**, a bulk topological excitation is precisely an edge excitation *x* ∈ X that can be moved into the bulk.

1. An edge excitation that can be moved inside the bulk must be equipped with a halfbraiding with all edge excitations in X. More explicitly, a bulk excitation can be realized by an edge excitation *x*, together with a family of isomorphisms

$$x \otimes y \xrightarrow{\beta_{x,y}} y \otimes x, \qquad \forall y \in \mathcal{X},$$
 (4.1)

such that the following diagrams

<span id="page-22-2"></span>
$$\begin{array}{ccc}
x \otimes y & \xrightarrow{\beta_{x,y}} & y \otimes x \\
\downarrow f_1 & & \forall f \in \text{hom}_{\mathfrak{X}}(y,z) \\
x \otimes z & \xrightarrow{\beta_{x,z}} & z \otimes x
\end{array} \tag{4.2}$$

are commutative. This family of isomorphisms β*x*,<sup>−</sup> = {β*x*,*y*}*y*∈<sup>X</sup> defines a natural isomorphism β*x*,<sup>−</sup> : *x* ⊗ − → − ⊗ *x*, which is called a half-braiding. Therefore, the pair (*x*, β*x*,<sup>−</sup>) defines a bulk excitation.

2. Moreover, morphisms (or instantons) between two bulk excitations (*x*, β*x*,<sup>−</sup>) and (*y*, β*y*,<sup>−</sup>) are precisely those morphisms (instantons) *f* ∈ homX(*x*, *y*) respecting the half-braiding, i.e. rending the following diagrams commutative:

$$\begin{array}{ccc}
x \otimes z & \xrightarrow{\beta_{x,z}} z \otimes x \\
f \otimes 1 & & \downarrow 1 \otimes f & \forall z \in \mathcal{X}. \\
y \otimes z & \xrightarrow{\beta_{y,z}} z \otimes y
\end{array} \tag{4.3}$$

All such pairs form a category, which is precisely the Drinfeld center Z(X) of X. The boundarybulk relation says that C ' Z(X) as UMTC's. We need generalize these arguments to the gapless edge (*V*, <sup>B</sup>X).

Now we consider a chiral gapless edge as depicted in Figure 9 (a), where x is a bulk topological excitation and y, z are two edge excitations. We should expect again that a bulk excitation can be realized by an edge excitation, together with a "half-braiding", a notion which will be made precise below.

1. An edge excitation  $x \in \mathcal{X}$  can be moved into the bulk if it is equipped with a half-braiding, which should consist of the following isomorphisms in  $\mathcal{X}$ :

<span id="page-23-0"></span>
$$\beta_{x,y}: x \otimes y \xrightarrow{\simeq} y \otimes x, \qquad \forall y \in \mathcal{X}.$$
 (4.4)

Moreover, they should satisfy a similar naturalness condition as in (4.2). Namely,  $\beta_{x,-}$ :  $x \otimes - \to - \otimes x$  should be a natural isomorphism between two endo-functor of  $\mathfrak{X}$ . But this condition is not enough because  $\hom_{\mathfrak{X}}(y,z)$  contains only the vacuum channels of the whole physical hom space  $[y,z]_{\mathfrak{B}}$ .

Note that a half-braiding is an adiabatic process of moving the bulk excitation x around an edge excitation  $y \in \mathcal{X}$ . This move automatically moves all observables on the world line supported on x. What observables could live on this world line in the bulk? It has to be a subspace of the boundary CFT  $[x,x]_{\mathbb{B}}$ . If this subspace is zero, then it is reasonable to say that x is not equipped with any half-braiding. The minimal requirement for a non-zero edge excitation x to move into the bulk is that the vacuum state in the boundary CFT  $[x,x]_{\mathbb{B}}$  survives on the world line in the bulk. This vacuum state is characterized by the canonical morphism  $\mathrm{id}_x: \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{B}} \to [x,x]_{\mathbb{B}}$  under the assumption that the chiral symmetry  $V = \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{B}}$  is preserved. This vacuum state can be fused into the space of observables on the world line supported on the edge, say  $[y,z]_{\mathbb{B}}$ , along a path from the bulk to the edge. As illustrated in Figure 9 (a), it is clear that this fusion should be path independent. Namely, we can fuse it into  $[y,z]_{\mathbb{B}}$  from left or first half-braid it to the right then fuse it from right without making any difference. This leads to the following commutative diagram:

<span id="page-23-1"></span>
$$[y,z]_{\mathbb{B}} \xrightarrow{1 \otimes \mathrm{id}_{x}} [y,z]_{\mathbb{B}} \otimes [x,x]_{\mathbb{B}} \xrightarrow{\otimes} [y \otimes x,z \otimes x]_{\mathbb{B}}$$

$$\downarrow^{-\circ\beta_{x,y}} \qquad \qquad \downarrow^{-\circ\beta_{x,y}} \qquad (4.5)$$

$$[x,x]_{\mathbb{B}} \otimes [y,z]_{\mathbb{B}} \xrightarrow{\otimes} [x \otimes y,x \otimes z]_{\mathbb{B}} \xrightarrow{\beta_{x,z} \circ -} [x \otimes y,z \otimes x]_{\mathbb{B}}.$$

The data (4.4) and the condition (4.5) give the precisely meaning of a "half-braiding" for a gapless edge. Therefore, such a pair  $(x, \beta_{x,-})$  should define a bulk excitation.

**Remark 4.16.** We conjecture that the complete boundary-bulk relation for *n*d topological orders with gapped/gapless boundaries and higher codimensional gapped/gapless defects on the boundary can also be stated as a symmetric monoidal equivalence of higher monoidal categories. This generalizes a conjecture proposed in [KWZ1] for *n*d topological orders with only gapped boundaries and gapped higher codimensional defects on the boundary.

### <span id="page-32-0"></span>5.2 Classification of non-chiral gapless edges

It turns out that  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$  is not the most general non-chiral symmetry. The algebraic structure on  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$  is not a VOA but a so-called full field algebra of central charges  $(c_L, c_R)$ , where  $c_L$  (resp.  $c_R$ ) is called the chiral (resp. anti-chiral) central charge, or just a full field algebra for simplicity [HK2, Ko1]. Let  $V_L$  and  $V_R$  be two (unitary) rational VOA's of central charges  $c_L$  and  $c_R$ , respectively. We will be interested in the so-called full field algebras over  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$ , which is a certain full field algebra of central charge  $(c_L, c_R)$  containing  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$  as a subalgebra (see [HK2, Definition 1.17] and texts below [HK2, Proposition 1.21]). The following theorem is a partial result proved in [Ko1, Theorem 4.15].

**Theorem 5.2.** A full field algebra (of central charges  $(c_L, c_R)$ ) over  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$  is equivalent to a commutative algebra in  $Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{Mod_{V_R}}$ .

In this work, by a non-chiral symmetry, we mean a unitary rational full field algebra. We provide a working definition of this notion below.

**Definition 5.3.** A full field algebra W of central charges  $(c_L, c_R)$  is called unitary rational if there exist two unitary rational VOA's  $V_L$  and  $V_R$  of central charges  $c_L$  and  $c_R$ , respectively, such that  $\text{Mod}_{V_L}$  and  $\text{Mod}_{V_R}$  are UMTC's, and W is a full field algebra over  $V_L \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \overline{V}_R$ , and, as a commutative algebra in  $\text{Mod}_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{\text{Mod}_{V_R}}$ , it is connected and separable (i.e. condensable [Ko3]).

It is possible to give a direct definition of the notion of a module over a full field algebra W such that the category  $Mod_W$  of W-modules is equivalent to  $(Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{Mod_{V_R}})_W^0$ , which denotes the category of local W-modules in  $Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{Mod_{V_R}}$ . For the purpose of this work, we can simply set  $Mod_W := (Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{Mod_{V_R}})_W^0$ . This definition is independent of the choices of  $V_L$  and  $V_R$ .

**Remark 5.4.** A condensable algebra in  $Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes Mod_{V_R}$  is automatically equipped with a canonical structure of a simple special symmetric  $\dagger$ -Frobenius algebra ( $\dagger$ -SSFA) in  $Mod_{V_L} \boxtimes \overline{Mod_{V_R}}$  (see for example [Ko3]).

Let  $\mathcal{B} := \operatorname{Mod}_W$  for a unitary rational full field algebra W of central charge  $(c_L, c_R)$ . Then  $(\mathcal{B}, c_L - c_R)$  defines a 2d topological order, and  $(W, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{B})$  defines the canonical non-chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{B}, c_L - c_R)$  with a non-chiral symmetry W.

By fusing canonical non-chiral gapless edges with some gapped walls, we obtain more general non-chiral gapless edges. Let X by a gapped wall between two 2d topological orders  $(\mathfrak{B}, c_L - c_R)$  and  $(\mathfrak{C}, c_L - c_R)$ . Then the following fusion formula:

$$\left(W,\,^{\mathcal{B}}\mathfrak{X}\right)=\left(W,\,^{\mathcal{B}}\mathfrak{B}\right)\boxtimes_{\left(\mathcal{B},\,c_{L}-c_{R}\right)}\left(\mathbb{C},\,^{\mathbf{H}}\mathfrak{X}\right)$$

defines a non-chiral gapless edge of  $(\mathcal{C}, c_L - c_R)$ . All of these non-chiral gapless edges can also be obtained from topological Wick rotations.

Sometimes, a non-chiral gapless edge can be gapped out. In this case, its bulk is a non-chiral 2d topological order. Non-chiral gapless edges of a non-chiral 2d topological order are always gappable. We gives some non-trivial examples.

<span id="page-32-1"></span>**Example 5.5.** Let **Is** be the Ising UMTC given by  $\operatorname{Mod}_{V_{\mathbf{Is}}}$ , where  $V_{\mathbf{Is}}$  is the well known Ising VOA with the central charge  $c=\frac{1}{2}$ . We have  $\Im(\mathbf{Is}) \simeq \mathbf{Is} \boxtimes \overline{\mathbf{Is}}$ . Let **Tor** be the UMTC describing the  $\mathbb{Z}_2$  2d topological order. It has four simple objects 1, e, m, f with the fusion rule given by  $e \otimes e = m \otimes m = f \otimes f = 1$  and  $m \otimes e = f$ . It is known that  $\mathbf{Tor} = \Im(\operatorname{Rep}(\mathbb{Z}_2))$ , where

$$W = \mathbf{1} \boxtimes \mathbf{1} \oplus \psi \boxtimes \psi, \tag{5.4}$$

The UFC  $(\Im(\mathbf{Is}))_W$  describes a gapped wall between  $(\Im(\mathbf{Is}),0)$  and  $(\mathbf{Tor},0)$ . $$\mathfrak{Z}(\mathfrak{Z}^{(\mathbf{Is})}(\mathfrak{Z}(\mathbf{Is}))_W) \simeq \mathbf{Tor}.$$

<span id="page-34-0"></span>**Theorem**ph **5.9.** Non-chiral gapless edges of a 2d topological order (C, *c*) are mathematically described and classified by pairs (*W*, <sup>B</sup>X), where

- <sup>B</sup>X is the enriched monoidal category defined by the pair (B, X) via the canonical construction, where B := Mod*<sup>W</sup>* and X is a closed fusion B-C-bimodule.

For the convenience of numerical computation, one can replace the pair (*W*, <sup>B</sup>X) by a new pair (*W*, *A*), where *A* is a Lagrangian algebra in B -C.

### <span id="page-35-0"></span>5.3 Purely edge phase transitions

We have mentioned that different pairs  $(W, {}^{\mathcal{B}}\mathcal{X})$  and  $(W', {}^{\mathcal{B}'}\mathcal{X}')$  should represent different nonchiral gapless edges. It means that if we deform one edge by adding perturbations to get the other one, we need go through at least one phase transition points. We do not have a physical proof of this claim. Actually, as far as we known, there is no universal or model-independent definition of a phase transition between two gapless phases. As we have already pointed out for chiral gapless edges in [KZ4], our mathematical theory of gapless edges actually provides such definitions. These definitions can automatically be generalized to include non-chiral gapless edges. More precisely, we propose that a 1+1D purely edge phase transition between two gapless chiral (resp. non-chiral) edges can be defined either

- 1. as a process of changing or breaking chiral (resp. non-chiral) symmetries; or
- 2. as the topological Wick rotation of a 2d topological phase transition, which is defined by a process of closing the gap, as illustrated in Figure 14.

**Remark 5.18.** We do not know how to generalize the first definition to higher dimensional gapless phases because the replacement for chiral or non-chiral symmetries is not so clear in higher dimensions.<sup>5</sup> But the second definition can be automatically generalized to higher dimensions. This provides a surprising and exciting implications to the study of higher dimensional gapless phases.

Actually, the story about purely edge phase transitions between chiral gapless edges become complete only when we include all non-chiral gapless edges because non-chiral gapless modes should appear at the critical points even if the initial and final edges are either chiral or gapped. For example, we can consider purely edge phase transitions between two gapped edges of a non-chiral topological order  $(\mathcal{C}, 0)$ . At the critical point, the gap is closed and necessarily non-chiral. Therefore,

<span id="page-35-2"></span> $<sup>^5</sup>$ We expect that an n-dimensional local quantum symmetries should be an analogue of an  $E_n$ -algebra.

For a given 2d topological order, it is an important problem to work out the complete phase diagram of all edges. #### <span id="page-49-0"></span>7 Conclusions and outlooks

In this work and [KZ4], we have developed the mathematical theory of gapped/gapless edges of 2d topological orders and higher codimensional gapped/gapless defects based on enriched (multi-)fusion categories and their representations. In this section, we discuss a few lessons we have learned from these two works.

The first lesson is that the mathematical description of a potentially anomalous gapped/gapless phase X depending on its codimension with respect to an anomaly-free topological order, in which X is realized as a defect with a non-trivial codimension [KWZ1]. For example, an anomaly-free 1+1D modular invariant RCFT has a precise mathematical description (see for

example [\[Ko2,](#page-52-18) Theorem 4.17]), which is a 0-codimensional description. If we want to regard it as a boundary of the trivial 2d topological order, then we need add all possible defects that are allowed by the local quantum symmetries (i.e. a non-chiral symmetry in this case). These defects form an enriched fusion category, which provides a 1-codimensional description of the anomaly-free 1+1D modular invariant RCFT. Moreover, the center of this enriched fusion category is precisely the 0-codimensional description of the trivial 2d topological order. We believe that this is a special case of a general principle for topological orders and its gapped/gapless boundaries in all dimensions.

**Boundary-bulk relation**: The center of the 1-codimensional categorical description, which contains all possible topological defects that can be obtained from elementary ones via condensations (called condensation descendants [\[KLWZZ\]](#page-52-19)), of a gapped/gapless boundary of an anomaly-free *n*d topological order X coincides with the 0-codimensional categorical description of X.

See more discussion in [\[KLWZZ,](#page-52-19) Section 3.3] and mathematical motivations in [\[GJF\]](#page-51-19).

The second lesson is that the study of gapped phases is that of gapless phases in disguise. Indeed, a general gapless phase can be obtained by stacking a gapless phase with a gapped phase. Therefore, the mathematical structure of a gapped phase, such as the higher category of topological excitations [\[KWZ1,](#page-52-2) [JF,](#page-52-20) [KLWZZ\]](#page-52-19), is also an indispensable ingredient of that of a generic gapless phase. This structure might be changed if we introduce interactions between two stacked layers of gapped and gapless phases, but its higher categorical nature remains intact. On the other hand, instead of using stacking, we can also describe this structure intrinsically. For a potentially anomalous gapless phase, it is possible to have gapped excitations, which are topological sectors of the complete Hilbert space. These topological sectors should form a higher categorical structure similar to those topological excitations in a topological order. We will call this higher categorical structure the "topological skeleton" of the gapless phase.

• For example, in the triple (*V*, *X*, <sup>P</sup>M) described in Theoremph [5.23,](#page-38-0) the enriched category <sup>P</sup>M is the topological skeleton. For a complete mathematical description of the gapless phase, one need add local quantum symmetries to the topological skeleton, such as *V* and *X* in the triple (*V*, *X*, <sup>P</sup>M).

We believe that this example has revealed the general features of gapless phases in all dimensions. More precisely, let us consider an anomalous gapped/gapless *n*D phase X*<sup>n</sup>* realized as a defect in an (*n* + *k*)D anomaly-free gapped/gapless phase Y*n*+*<sup>k</sup>* . More precisely, X*<sup>n</sup>* should be viewed as an *n*D domain wall between two *n*+1D defects S*n*+<sup>1</sup> and T*n*+<sup>1</sup> in Y*n*+*<sup>k</sup>* , so on and so forth. There is an *n*D local quantum symmetry *V<sup>n</sup>* defined on X*n*, where *V<sup>n</sup>* is a (topological or conformal or geometrical) analogue of an *En*-algebra and will be called an *n*-disk algebra. There should be an *n*+1D local quantum symmetry *V<sup>n</sup>*+<sup>1</sup> defined in the *n*+1D neighborhood of X*<sup>n</sup>* determined by S*<sup>n</sup>*+<sup>1</sup> and T*<sup>n</sup>*+1, where *V<sup>n</sup>*+<sup>1</sup> is an (*n*+1)-disk algebra and a common subalgebra of two (*n*+1)-disk algebras that define the *n*+1D local quantum symmetries in S*<sup>n</sup>*+<sup>1</sup> and T*<sup>n</sup>*+1, respectively. Moreover, *V<sup>n</sup>* is an *n*-disk algebra over *V<sup>n</sup>*+<sup>1</sup> in the sense of Lurie [\[Lu\]](#page-53-1). So on and so forth. As a consequence, we expect a *k*-codimensional mathematical description of X*<sup>n</sup>* to be given by (*V<sup>n</sup>*+*<sup>k</sup>* , · · · , *Vn*, X ] ), where X ] is the topological skeleton.

In this context, the Gapped-Gapless Correspondence proposed in [\[KZ4,](#page-53-0) Section 7] can be restated as follows: the topological skeletons of gapless phases can all be obtained by topological Wick rotations from gapped phases. Also note that local quantum symmetries *V<sup>i</sup>* are not independent of and must be compatible with the topological skeletons, which provide a severe constraint to possible local quantum symmetries. In this sense, the topological skeleton can be viewed as a categorical symmetry of a gapless phase, and provides a powerful tool and a systematic way to study all gapless phases.
